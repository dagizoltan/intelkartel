<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The old-school gangster code of the 1840s often revolved around unwritten rules and honor among thieves, reflecting the values and practices of organized criminal elements from that era. These codes were shaped by societal norms, criminal subcultures, and localized gang structures.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Key Elements of Old-School Gangster Code (Circa 1840)</h3>
<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Loyalty Above All Else</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Core Principle:</strong> Absolute loyalty to the gang or group was paramount.</li>
<li>Members were expected to put the group’s interests above their own, even under threat of punishment or death.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Omertà (Code of Silence)</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Rule:</strong> Never betray the group or reveal its secrets to outsiders, especially law enforcement.</li>
<li>Informants or those suspected of treachery faced harsh penalties, often death.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Respect and Hierarchy</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Gang structures were strictly hierarchical, with clear lines of authority.</li>
<li>Members were expected to show respect to leaders and follow orders without question.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Honor Among Thieves</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Ethical Standards:</strong> Stealing or harming fellow members was strictly forbidden.</li>
<li>Violating this rule resulted in ostracization or worse.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Retribution</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Eye for an Eye:</strong> Any insult, betrayal, or harm to a member or the gang had to be avenged to maintain the group’s honor and reputation.</li>
<li>Feuds between rival groups often arose from perceived slights or breaches of trust.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Protection of the Vulnerable (Selective Morality)</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Certain gang cultures, especially those with ties to local communities, claimed to protect “their own” (e.g., poor residents or families in their area) while exploiting others.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Earnings Shared Equitably</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Profits from illegal activities were typically shared within the gang according to roles and hierarchy.</li>
<li>Skimming or withholding earnings was considered a grave offense.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Secrecy in Communication</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Members used coded language, slang, or symbols to communicate.</li>
<li>These codes were designed to be indecipherable to outsiders.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Initiation Rites</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Membership often required initiation, such as committing a crime to prove loyalty and capability.</li>
<li>Rituals served to bind the member to the group and ensure mutual culpability.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Neutral Ground Rules</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Certain locations (e.g., churches, markets, or agreed-upon safe zones) were designated as neutral and off-limits for gang activities or violence.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Examples from the Era</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>The Bowery Boys (New York):</strong> Known for their loyalty and dress code, they adhered to a sense of mutual protection and street justice.</li>
<li><strong>The Forty Thieves (New York):</strong> One of the earliest organized gangs in the U.S., they operated under strict loyalty and retribution rules.</li>
<li><strong>Peaky Blinders (UK):</strong> Though slightly later in origin, similar groups in the 1840s upheld these codes through dress, conduct, and community engagement.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">While much of this “code” was informal, it was enforced with ruthless consistency. These rules helped maintain order within criminal organizations and ensured their survival in volatile urban environments of the 19th century.</p>
