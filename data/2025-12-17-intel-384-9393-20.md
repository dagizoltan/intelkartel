<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Brats of the Algorithm</strong></p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Old money built museums. The <strong>nouveau riche</strong>, in this imagined 2025, built <em>systems</em> — ugly ones — and then stuffed their children inside them.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Lacking lineage, taste or restraint, this new elite did what insecure wealth always does: it overcompensated. Its fortunes, made quickly from platforms, crypto, defence-adjacent software and attention markets, could buy anything except legitimacy. So instead, it bought <em>influence without fingerprints</em>.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Their children became the solution.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Institutionalised, not educated</h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The nouveau riche did not raise their offspring. They <strong>institutionalised</strong> them.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">These children were shipped off to glossy “neuro-leadership academies”, “future cognition institutes” and “resilience campuses”, all marketed as elite alternatives to schooling. In reality, they were holding pens for privilege — places where moral development was quietly replaced by optimisation metrics.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Affection was scarce. Surveillance was constant. Emotional detachment was praised as maturity. By adolescence, these children were fluent in dashboards, behavioural graphs and psychological leverage — but illiterate in empathy.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The parents, busy laundering reputations through philanthropy, called this <em>preparing them for the future</em>.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">The ghost networks they unleashed</h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In this fictional world, the nouveau riche discovered that the cheapest way to frighten a population was not with police or prisons, but with <strong>memory itself</strong>.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Thus emerged the <strong>Digital Ghost Networks</strong>: sprawling systems of abandoned data, dead accounts, archived voices and scraped personalities. The internet, after all, never forgets — and with generative AI, it never shuts up either.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Who better to operate these networks than children raised without moral anchors?</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">They learned how to resurrect dead voices, simulate old friends, mimic lost relatives. A message from a “ghost” would arrive at just the wrong hour. A familiar face would appear in a feed, saying something it never would have said — except now it did.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The effect was not panic, but <em>unease</em>. The sort that corrodes trust in one’s own mind.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The nouveau riche loved this approach. It was elegant. It was deniable. And above all, it did not make them look like the bullies they were.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Terror, but make it passive-aggressive</h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Violence is messy. Laws attract lawyers. But dreams? Dreams leave no bruises.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">So the institutions moved deeper — into sleep.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Dream-induction technology in this imagined 2025 was sold as wellness: sleep optimisation, lucid dreaming, trauma relief. In practice, it was behavioural nudging taken to its logical extreme. Wearables tracked neural rhythms. Audio micro-stimuli adapted in real time. AI systems learned which symbols triggered fear, compliance or paralysis.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The institutionalised children became its curators. They designed nightmares the way marketers design campaigns.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">No orders were given. No slogans appeared. People simply woke up anxious, watched, diminished. Too tired to resist. Too unsettled to organise.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The nouveau riche called this <em>non-violent stabilisation</em>.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Everyone else called it hell — though never out loud, because even naming it felt dangerous.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">A class without taste, ethics or brakes</h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">What distinguishes the nouveau riche in this speculative future is not wealth, but <strong>crudeness</strong>. They did not inherit norms. They did not absorb limits. They mistook technical capability for moral permission.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Where old elites feared scandal, these ones feared only irrelevance.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Their children — emotionally stunted, algorithmically gifted — became tools of that fear. Not masterminds, not rebels, but well-trained extensions of their parents’ insecurity.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">If there is a villain here, it is not technology. It is a class that confused money with wisdom, and optimisation with virtue.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">A warning dressed as fiction</h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This imagined 2025 exaggerates, but only slightly. Data already outlives people. AI already imitates them. Sleep is already monitored. Childhood is already commodified.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The danger lies not in innovation, but in <strong>who controls it — and how little shame they possess</strong>.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In this story, terror wears the face of a startup demo. Oppression sounds like a wellness app. And the children of the vulgar rich inherit not a world, but a weapon — and are praised for pulling the trigger while being told it isn’t one.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The ghosts are real enough.<br/>The dreams are optional.<br/>And the fear, as always, trickles down.</p>
