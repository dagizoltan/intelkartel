<h3 class="wp-block-heading">1. <strong>Falling Back Behind the Dnipro River</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Historically, falling back across a major river involves:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Phase Withdrawal</strong>: Done in stages to avoid collapse.</li>
<li><strong>Rear Guard Actions</strong>: Units delay the enemy to cover retreat.</li>
<li><strong>Bridge and Ferry Management</strong>: Secure crossings or destroy them after use.</li>
<li><strong>Artillery Cover</strong>: Fire support during withdrawal and to slow pursuit.</li>
<li><strong>Camouflage and Deception</strong>: Mask movements and create dummy positions.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">2. <strong>Constructing a 7-Line Defense (Doctrinal Framework)</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This is often layered as:</p>
<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Forward Security Zone</strong>: Recon, sensors, minor units.</li>
<li><strong>Disruption Zone</strong>: Obstacles (mines, traps), limited forces.</li>
<li><strong>Main Defensive Belt (1st Line)</strong>: Infantry trenches, strongpoints, ATGMs.</li>
<li><strong>Secondary Line</strong>: Reinforced bunkers, armor reserves.</li>
<li><strong>Artillery Fire Zone</strong>: Interdiction of enemy logistics/reserves.</li>
<li><strong>Logistical Rear</strong>: Supply, command, repair, evacuation.</li>
<li><strong>Counterattack Staging Area</strong>: Mechanized reserves.</li>
</ol>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">To cover a <strong>20 km deep kill zone</strong>, you’d typically use:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Artillery and MLRS</strong> with 20–70 km range (e.g., HIMARS, PzH 2000, M109, or Soviet 2S19).</li>
<li><strong>UAVs and Counter-battery radars</strong> to locate and target Russian artillery.</li>
<li><strong>Mines and remote sensor fields</strong> (NATO standard can involve over 3,000 mines per km² in dense layouts).</li>
</ul>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">3. <strong>Estimating Artillery Shell Needs to Match Russian Capability</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Russia reportedly fires 10,000–20,000 shells per day (varies by sector). To match that in a 20 km zone:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Assume 3-4 battalions of artillery per 20 km front (18–24 guns per battalion)</strong></li>
<li>To sustain parity: <strong>~2,000–5,000 shells/day per 20 km of front</strong>, depending on intensity.</li>
<li>Ukraine would need:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>~60–150 shells per gun/day.</li>
<li><strong>Minimum monthly supply</strong>: 60,000–150,000 shells/20 km.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Modern NATO doctrine prefers <strong>precision over volume</strong>, so:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Use of Excalibur, Vulcano, or BONUS shells could reduce needed volume by 3–5x.</li>
</ul>
