<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Below is a <strong>comprehensive, high-level proposal</strong> for a diplomatic-security firm called <strong>Core Interest Defenses (C.I.D.)</strong>, modeled loosely on companies like <strong>Blackwater (now Constellis)</strong> and <strong>GJW Government Relations</strong>, and designed to appeal to transatlantic defense and security stakeholders — particularly <strong>Ukraine, Hungary, Israel, the U.S., and the U.K.</strong>.</p>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Core Interest Defenses (C.I.D.)</strong></h2>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Strategic Proposal</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>“Securing the Legacy of Freedom — Defending the Future of Democratic Capitalism.”</strong></p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>I. Executive Summary</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Core Interest Defenses (C.I.D.)</strong> is a strategic security-diplomatic and private military firm dedicated to safeguarding the national, economic, and historical interests of the transatlantic democratic bloc. Formed by defense experts, former intelligence officers, private equity strategists, and senior military veterans, C.I.D. exists to protect those who contributed to — and still benefit from — the democratic transition, privatization, and Western integration of the former Soviet sphere (1988–2025).</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Our mission is clear: <strong>defend the architects, operatives, corporate actors, and state allies</strong> responsible for securing the global liberal-democratic order established after the Cold War, particularly against renewed authoritarian threats and revisionist regimes.</p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>II. Strategic Rationale</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">From 1988 to 2025, a transatlantic and regional alliance catalyzed the <strong>democratization of the USSR</strong>, the <strong>privatization of its economies</strong>, and the <strong>expansion of NATO, the EU, and global markets</strong>. The success of this unipolar moment has come under direct threat from:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Neo-imperial Russia</strong></li>
<li><strong>State-sponsored revisionism (China/Iran)</strong></li>
<li><strong>Hybrid warfare, cyber disruption, and mercenary-state actors</strong></li>
<li><strong>Assassinations, disinformation, and soft subversion of allied governments</strong></li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">C.I.D. is designed to <strong>counter these threats using sovereign-agnostic, pro-Western force projection</strong>, intelligence synchronization, and strategic lobbying.</p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>III. Core Clients &amp; Strategic Partners</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">C.I.D. supports and aligns with:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Ukraine:</strong> Protection of post-Maidan democratic operators, oligarchs-turned-liberal-investors, and reformers from Russian retaliation.</li>
<li><strong>Hungary:</strong> Discreet counterbalance to internal autocratic drift; defend Hungarian Western-aligned capital, technocrats, and free-market actors.</li>
<li><strong>Israel:</strong> Liaison and operations support for regional force protection, cyber-espionage defense, and strategic counterintelligence.</li>
<li><strong>United Kingdom &amp; United States:</strong> High-level strategic lobbying, PMC operations in contested regions, and private-state defense linkages.</li>
<li><strong>Private sector clients:</strong> Corporations with assets acquired through post-Soviet privatization, vulnerable to state or corporate expropriation.</li>
</ul>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

</ul>

</ul>

</ul>
