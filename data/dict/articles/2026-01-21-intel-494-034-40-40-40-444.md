<h2 class="wp-block-heading">High-Level Digital Investigation Framework</h2>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">(Hungarian Legal Context – Authority Use Only)</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Legal Basis (Hungary)</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Investigative actions involving communications, internet data, or surveillance in Hungary are governed primarily by:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Act XC of 2017 on the Code of Criminal Procedure (Be.)</strong></li>



<li><strong>Act C of 2012 on the Criminal Code (Btk.)</strong></li>



<li><strong>GDPR &amp; Hungarian data-protection law</strong></li>



<li>Judicial authorization requirements (court or prosecutor, depending on measure)</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Private individuals <strong>may not</strong> conduct surveillance, access private systems, or collect non-public data.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">1. Case Initiation Threshold</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Authorities may proceed only after:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>A <strong>report (feljelentés)</strong> is received in good faith, OR</li>



<li>Credible information suggests a crime under the <strong>Btk.</strong> (e.g., coercion, abuse, exploitation of minors).</li>
</ul>



<blockquote class="wp-block-quote is-layout-flow wp-block-quote-is-layout-flow">
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Allegations alone do not establish guilt; they establish a <strong>basis to assess</strong>.</p>
</blockquote>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">2. Permissible Open-Source Review (OSINT)</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Without a warrant</strong>, authorities may review only:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Public websites</li>



<li>Public social-media posts</li>



<li>Public advertisements or event listings</li>



<li>Public company registry data</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Requirements:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Documentation of URLs, timestamps, and context</li>



<li>No interaction with suspects</li>



<li>No deception or entrapment</li>
</ul>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">3. Source Reliability Assessment</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Authorities classify sources as:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Official</strong> (registries, inspections, court records)</li>



<li><strong>Media</strong> (licensed journalism)</li>



<li><strong>Open online claims</strong> (forums, anonymous posts – low reliability)</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Unverified online claims <strong>cannot</strong> be treated as evidence on their own.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">4. Escalation Criteria</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">If OSINT reveals indicators suggesting:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Violence, coercion, or deprivation of liberty</strong>, or</li>



<li><strong>Sexual exploitation, especially involving minors</strong></li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Then authorities may:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Open a formal investigation</li>



<li>Notify specialized units (e.g., child-protection, organized crime)</li>
</ul>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">5. Judicially Authorized Digital Measures</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><em>(Authorities only – requires court/prosecutor approval under Be.)</em></p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Possible measures (strictly limited and targeted):</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Requests to hosting providers or platforms</li>



<li>Traffic or subscriber data requests</li>



<li>Search and seizure of digital devices</li>



<li>Communication interception (only for serious crimes)</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">All measures must satisfy:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Necessity</strong></li>



<li><strong>Proportionality</strong></li>



<li><strong>Specific scope and timeframe</strong></li>
</ul>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">6. Digital Forensics &amp; Chain of Custody</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">If devices or data are lawfully obtained:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Forensic imaging by certified experts</li>



<li>Evidence integrity maintained</li>



<li>Clear documentation for court review</li>
</ul>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">7. Corroboration Requirement</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Digital findings must be corroborated with:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Financial records (lawfully obtained)</li>



<li>Licensing and inspection records</li>



<li>Witness or victim statements</li>



<li>Physical evidence, if any</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">No prosecution proceeds on internet data alone.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">8. Victim Protection Obligations</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">If risk to individuals is identified:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Immediate safeguarding measures</li>



<li>Confidential handling of sensitive information</li>



<li>Special protections for minors under Hungarian law</li>
</ul>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">9. Prosecutorial Review</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Before any charges:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Evidence reviewed by the <strong>prosecutor’s office</strong></li>



<li>Legal sufficiency assessed</li>



<li>Decision made on continuation, dismissal, or indictment</li>
</ul>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">10. Prohibitions (Critical)</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Under Hungarian law, <strong>private individuals must not</strong>:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Hack, surveil, or monitor internet use</li>



<li>Access private systems or accounts</li>



<li>Publish unverified criminal accusations</li>



<li>Conduct “sting” operations</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Such actions may themselves be criminal.</p>



<h1 class="wp-block-heading">Lawful Digital Investigation Protocol (Authority Use Only)</h1>

<ul class="wp-block-list">

<li><strong>Case predicate established</strong> (credible complaint, tip, or corroborated indicators).</li>



<li><strong>Judicial authorization</strong> obtained <em>before</em> any non-public data collection.</li>



<li><strong>Child-protection escalation</strong> if minors are plausibly implicated.</li>
</ul>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">2. Evidence Preservation (Non-Intrusive)</h2>



<ul class="wp-block-list">

<li>Official websites, public social media posts, public ads, public reviews.</li>
</ul>
</li>



<li>Capture with <strong>forensic integrity</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Timestamped screenshots, URL logs, hash values.</li>



<li>Archive tools that preserve metadata.</li>
</ul>
</li>



<li><strong>No interaction</strong> with subjects (no entrapment, no messaging).</li>
</ul>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">3. Open-Source Intelligence (OSINT) Review</h2>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Business footprint analysis:
<ul class="wp-block-list">

<li>Hosting provider and IP ranges (high-level, non-intrusive).</li>



<li>Public marketing patterns, event listings, public job ads.</li>
</ul>
</li>



<li>Consistency checks:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Compare claims across platforms for discrepancies.</li>



<li>Identify <em>patterns</em>, not conclusions.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">4. Threshold Assessment</h2>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Determine whether OSINT reveals:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Indicators of organized criminal activity.</li>



<li>Indicators triggering child-safety statutes.</li>
</ul>
</li>



<li>If threshold met → <strong>formal investigation</strong> opened.</li>
</ul>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">5. Judicially Authorized Data Requests</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><em>(Authorities only; requires warrants/subpoenas)</em></p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Requests to:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>ISPs, hosting providers, email platforms, payment processors.</li>
</ul>
</li>



<li>Scope strictly limited to:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Timeframes relevant to the predicate.</li>



<li>Accounts or infrastructure tied by evidence.</li>
</ul>
</li>



<li><strong>Minimization rules</strong> applied to avoid over-collection.</li>
</ul>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">6. Digital Forensics</h2>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>If devices/accounts are lawfully seized:
<ul class="wp-block-list">

<li>Forensic imaging by certified examiners.</li>



<li>Keyword, communication, and transaction analysis per warrant scope.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">7. Corroboration &amp; Victim Safeguards</h2>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Corroborate digital findings with:
<ul class="wp-block-list">

<li>Witness statements.</li>
</ul>
</li>



<li>Immediate <strong>victim protection measures</strong> if risk is identified.</li>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">8. Reporting &amp; Review</h2>



<ul class="wp-block-list">

<ul class="wp-block-list">

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">9. Ethics &amp; Compliance</h2>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>THE ABERLATH PROTOCOL</strong></h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Budapest, 2041</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The city no longer slept.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Under the Danube, beneath thermal caverns older than the language spoken above them, the <strong>Averlath Signal</strong> pulsed—once every eleven minutes, a rhythm hidden inside civilian networks, unnoticed by ordinary systems. It wasn’t a virus. It wasn’t malware.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">It was influence.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Captain Réka Szalai of the <strong>Budapest Cyber–Crimes and Strategic Threat Unit (BCSTU)</strong> stared at the holographic lattice hovering over the briefing table. Every glowing node represented a human decision subtly altered—votes changed, loyalties redirected, identities fractured.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">“This isn’t espionage,” she said quietly. “It’s social erosion.”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The Averlath Collective had no flag, no army, no ideology on paper. It preyed on fault lines—fear, shame, secrecy. Its weapon was not force, but <strong>leverage</strong>.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">And Hungary was the test ground.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Phase I: Detection</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The first clue came from a pattern no AI should have noticed: an anomaly in public Wi-Net traffic near nightlife districts—micro-bursts of encrypted data riding harmless entertainment streams.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Lieutenant Márton Fekete, the unit’s systems architect, traced the signal to a distributed mesh buried deep in legacy infrastructure.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">“Whoever built this,” he said, “understands human weakness better than code.”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The Collective wasn’t hacking people’s devices.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">It was hacking <strong>their secrets</strong>.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Phase II: Containment</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The Hungarian Parliament authorized the <strong>Averlath Protocol</strong>—a classified defensive operation permitted only when national cohesion itself was under threat.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">No mass surveillance.<br>No collective punishment.<br>No shadows without warrants.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Instead, BCSTU deployed <strong>MirrorNet</strong>—a counter-system designed to reflect influence back to its source without exposing civilians.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The challenge was surgical precision.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">If they acted too broadly, they would fracture trust.<br>Too narrowly, and the Collective would disappear again.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Phase III: The Descent</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The signal origin wasn’t a building.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">It was a <strong>space</strong>—an abandoned thermal research hub sealed after the 2029 floods, now invisible to standard mapping systems. Below the city’s lights, beneath layers of history, the Collective had built its core.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Not a base.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">A <strong>theater</strong>.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Walls of adaptive screens replayed fragments of stolen lives—edited, distorted, weaponized. The Averlath philosophy was simple: <em>control the narrative, and you control the future.</em></p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Szalai felt the weight of it as the team advanced.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">“This ends tonight,” she said.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Phase IV: The Takedown</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">MirrorNet went live.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Instead of shutting the system down, BCSTU fed it truth—uncorrupted data, anonymized but undeniable. Financial flows. Fabricated leverage exposed. Artificial personas collapsing under their own contradictions.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The Collective had never faced resistance that didn’t play by its rules.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Within minutes, the influence lattice began to fold inward.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Averlath was not destroyed.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">It was <strong>rendered powerless</strong>.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Phase V: Aftermath</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">By morning, Budapest woke unchanged—and that was the victory.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">No panic.<br>No headlines.<br>No heroes.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Just a city that remained <strong>itself</strong>.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In a sealed report to Parliament, Szalai wrote:</p>



<blockquote class="wp-block-quote is-layout-flow wp-block-quote-is-layout-flow">
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">“The greatest threat was never the system, but the idea that people could be reduced to secrets. We defended Hungary not by force, but by refusing to become what opposed us.”</p>
</blockquote>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">As the Danube flowed on, Réka Szalai stepped onto the bridge at dawn, watching light break across the city.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">For now, Budapest was safe.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">But somewhere, deep in the noise of the world, new signals were always forming.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">And the Protocol would be ready.</p>
