<h2 class="wp-block-heading">LAST COMMUNISMS ON THE PLANET: </h2>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>MILITARY INTELLIGENCE MEMORANDUM</strong><br/><strong>SUBJECT:</strong> Comparative Statistics on Global Communist Populations and Party Membership (1949–2023)</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>DATE:</strong> January 19, 2025</p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>I. POPULATION LIVING UNDER COMMUNIST RULE</strong></p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>1949:</strong> ~540 million people (primarily USSR, China, and Eastern Bloc nations).</li>
<li><strong>Peak (Late 20th Century):</strong> ~1.5 billion people.</li>
<li><strong>2023:</strong> ~1.4 billion people (China, Vietnam, Laos, Cuba, North Korea).</li>
</ul>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>II. COMMUNIST PARTY MEMBERSHIP</strong></p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>1949 (CPC – China):</strong> ~4.5 million members.</li>
<li><strong>2023 (CPC – China):</strong> ~99.19 million members (7.04% of China’s population).</li>
<li><strong>Other Parties (2023):</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Vietnam:</strong> ~5.2 million members (~5.4% of population).</li>
<li><strong>Cuba:</strong> ~800,000 members (~7% of population).</li>
<li><strong>North Korea:</strong> ~6 million members (~25% of population, including associated youth leagues).</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>III. HISTORICAL TREND SUMMARY</strong></p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Peak Communist Membership (USSR, China, Eastern Bloc):</strong> Estimated 200+ million party members globally (1970s–1980s).</li>
<li><strong>Post-Soviet Decline:</strong> Membership reduced significantly in Eastern Europe and former USSR states.</li>
<li><strong>Current Total Membership (2023):</strong> Estimated ~112 million globally, with CPC comprising ~88.5%.</li>
</ul>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>IV. OBSERVATIONS</strong></p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Population Trends:</strong> Decline in global population under communism attributed to regime transitions in Europe and Central Asia.</li>
<li><strong>Party Membership Trends:</strong> Growth in CPC membership contrasts with global declines elsewhere.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">END OF MEMORANDUM</p>

<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The Torah’s prohibition against murder is deeply rooted in its theological, moral, and legal framework. Below is an extensive exploration of the reasons behind this prohibition, the consequences of murder in ancient times, and the concept of revenge:</p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Reasons Why the Torah Bans Murder</strong></h3>
<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Sanctity of Human Life</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Humans are created in the image of God (<em>Imago Dei</em>, Genesis 1:27). Taking a human life is seen as an affront to God Himself.</li>
<li>Life is a divine gift, and only God has the authority to determine its end.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Moral and Ethical Foundation</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Murder violates the moral order established by the Torah. It disrupts communal harmony and undermines trust among individuals.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Legal and Social Order</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Ancient societies, including those guided by Torah laws, required strict regulations to maintain stability. Murder was considered an act of chaos that threatened the foundation of society.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Violation of the Commandments</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The Sixth Commandment explicitly states: “You shall not murder” (<em>Lo Tirtzach</em>). This commandment underscores the Torah’s emphasis on the inviolability of life.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Justice and Accountability</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Murder denies individuals their right to justice and disrupts the communal sense of fairness.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Prohibition of Vengeance and Vigilantism</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The Torah discourages personal revenge, instead establishing a system of legal redress to handle such crimes.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Consequences of Murder in the Past</strong></h3>
<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Capital Punishment</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Murderers were often subject to the death penalty. For instance, Numbers 35:31 states that no ransom is accepted for the life of a murderer; they must be put to death.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>City of Refuge</strong> (<em>Arei Miklat</em>):
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>If the murder was unintentional, the perpetrator could flee to a city of refuge to avoid revenge by the victim’s family. This ensured a fair trial and protected against wrongful killings (Numbers 35:11-28).</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Restitution and Atonement</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>In cases of manslaughter, the offender might be required to live in exile within the city of refuge until the death of the High Priest, symbolizing the completion of atonement.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Divine Justice</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The Torah also highlights that ultimate justice lies with God. If human systems failed to punish a murderer, divine retribution was believed to follow.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Bloodguilt</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The land itself was considered defiled by murder, requiring justice to cleanse the community (Numbers 35:33-34).</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Revenge and Its Regulation</strong></h3>
<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Blood Avenger (Go’el HaDam)</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>In cases of murder, the nearest kin of the victim (called the <em>Go’el HaDam</em>) had the right to avenge the death. However, this right was strictly regulated to prevent an endless cycle of revenge (Numbers 35:19).</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Distinction Between Murder and Manslaughter</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Premeditated murder warranted the death penalty, whereas unintentional killing allowed for refuge, preventing revenge killings while maintaining justice.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Rejection of Collective Punishment</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The Torah emphasizes individual accountability. For example, Ezekiel 18:20 states: “The soul who sins shall die.” This principle discourages acts of revenge against the family or community of the offender.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Limitations on Retaliation</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>While the concept of <em>Lex Talionis</em> (“an eye for an eye”) exists, it is interpreted by Jewish tradition as advocating proportionality and legal oversight rather than literal retaliation.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Philosophical and Spiritual Implications</strong></h3>
<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Human Relationships</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Murder is a violation of the relational bonds God intended for humanity, as it destroys trust and unity.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Cycle of Violence</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The Torah seeks to end cycles of violence by advocating structured legal systems rather than personal vengeance.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Respect for Divine Authority</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Prohibiting murder emphasizes the belief that humans are not autonomous beings but accountable to God’s moral law.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Teaching Responsibility</strong>:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>By holding individuals accountable for taking life, the Torah teaches the gravity of moral responsibility in human actions.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In summary, the Torah bans murder to preserve the sanctity of life, maintain justice, and ensure communal stability. Its legal and moral frameworks aim to prevent the chaos of unregulated vengeance and provide a path toward justice and atonement, balancing human accountability with divine authority.</p>
