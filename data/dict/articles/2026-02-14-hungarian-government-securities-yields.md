<h2 class="wp-block-heading">📊 <strong>Clarifying the Difference: Nominal Coupons vs. Market Yields</strong></h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">It’s important to distinguish between two different figures:</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">🔹 <strong>Nominal Coupon Rates</strong></h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>These are the <em>interest rates printed on the bond</em> when first issued (what the issuer promised to pay annually).</li>



<li>Some Hungarian government bonds, <strong>particularly early‑issued Prémium Magyar Állampapír series</strong>, offered <em>very high coupon rates</em> (≥ 15 % nominal) — but these reflect specific past conditions at issuance, not current market performance.(<a href="https://aranyatveszek.hu/hu/hirek/csucs?utm_source=chatgpt.com">aranyatveszek.hu</a>)</li>
</ul>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">🔹 <strong>Market Yields</strong></h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Yield to maturity</strong> reflects the <em>real return investors expect based on current pricing in the market</em> — and these are what financial markets actively trade and quote.</li>



<li>Current and recent market yields for Hungary’s longer‑term bonds have remained in the <strong>~6 – 7 % range</strong> (far below 17 %).(<a href="https://tradingeconomics.com/hungary/government-bond-yield?utm_source=chatgpt.com">Trading Economics</a>)</li>
</ul>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">📌 <strong>Why Coupon Rates Can Be Much Higher Than Market Yields</strong></h2>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Government bonds that were issued during periods of very high interest rate environments <em>locked in high coupon rates</em>, which may still look attractive on paper.</li>



<li>However, as market conditions and demand change, the <strong>actual yields investors receive today</strong> (if purchased second‑hand or valued in the market) <em>move toward prevailing interest rate levels</em>.</li>



<li>For example, even a bond with a high coupon can trade at a premium (above face value), reducing the effective <em>yield to maturity</em> closer to current market yields. This means the <em>price investors pay may offset the high stated coupon</em>.</li>
</ul>
