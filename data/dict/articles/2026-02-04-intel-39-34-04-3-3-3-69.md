<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Purpose</strong></h2>



<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">This memorandum analyzes El Salvador’s current internal security strategy as a case study in <strong>coercive state consolidation</strong> against entrenched non‑state armed actors. It evaluates the strategy’s effectiveness, strategic logic, and implications for civil‑military relations, regional security, and long‑term state capacity. The assessment adopts a <strong>realist and hawkish analytical lens</strong>, prioritizing order, deterrence, and sovereignty over liberal governance norms.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Strategic Context</strong></h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">For decades, El Salvador exhibited characteristics of a <strong>partially failed internal security state</strong>:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Fragmented monopoly on violence</li>



<li>Criminal organizations exercising de facto territorial governance</li>



<li>Limited deterrence credibility</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The present strategy represents a <strong>deliberate rupture</strong> with post–Cold War security orthodoxy, rejecting incremental reform in favor of <strong>overwhelming coercive control</strong>. The state has redefined gangs not as criminal justice problems, but as <strong>internal enemies</strong> requiring suppression through exceptional measures.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Strategic Logic</strong></h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">El Salvador’s approach aligns with classic counter‑insurgency and authoritarian stabilization models:</p>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Primacy of Order</strong><br>Security is treated as a precondition for all political and economic activity. Civil liberties are subordinated to state survival.</li>



<li><strong>Deterrence Through Overmatch</strong><br>The state seeks to destroy not only gang capability, but gang <em>expectations</em> of survival, negotiation, or impunity.</li>



<li><strong>Centralization of Authority</strong><br>Executive dominance is justified as necessary to overcome institutional inertia and fragmented governance.</li>



<li><strong>Psychological Control of the Population</strong><br>Visible repression serves both adversary neutralization and population compliance.</li>
</ol>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">From a hawkish perspective, the strategy reflects an acceptance that <strong>legitimacy follows control</strong>, not the reverse.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Assessment of Effectiveness</strong></h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Strengths</strong></h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Restoration of the Monopoly on Violence</strong><br>The state has reasserted control over territory long dominated by criminal actors.</li>



<li><strong>Strategic Shock to Adversaries</strong><br>Criminal networks have been disrupted faster than adaptive learning cycles can respond.</li>



<li><strong>Domestic Political Dominance</strong><br>Popular support for security outcomes reduces internal friction and opposition constraints.</li>



<li><strong>Demonstration Effect</strong><br>Signals to both internal and regional actors that the state is willing to incur costs to enforce sovereignty.</li>
</ul>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Limitations and Risks</strong></h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Coercion Dependence</strong><br>The strategy’s success is contingent on sustained pressure; any relaxation risks rapid adversary regeneration.</li>



<li><strong>Institutional Militarization</strong><br>Long-term reliance on security forces for governance risks distorting civil‑military balance.</li>



<li><strong>Criminal Displacement, Not Eradication</strong><br>Networks may externalize violence into neighboring states or evolve into less visible forms.</li>



<li><strong>Strategic Isolation</strong><br>International criticism may constrain economic and security partnerships, though this is currently discounted by leadership.</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">From a hard‑power perspective, these risks are <strong>acceptable</strong> so long as internal dominance is preserved.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Comparative Strategic Framework</strong></h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">El Salvador’s strategy resembles historical cases of coercive stabilization:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Latin American internal security regimes (1970s–80s)</strong></li>



<li><strong>Post‑conflict authoritarian consolidation models</strong></li>



<li><strong>High‑intensity counter‑criminal state campaigns</strong></li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In each case, <strong>short‑term order was achieved through repression</strong>, while long‑term outcomes depended on whether coercion transitioned into institutionalized authority.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Strategic Implications</strong></h2>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>For El Salvador</strong><br>The state has decisively shifted from weak governance to <strong>command governance</strong>. The critical challenge is not success, but <strong>exit strategy</strong> from perpetual emergency rule.</li>



<li><strong>For the Region</strong><br>Neighboring states may face spillover pressures, incentivizing either imitation or containment.</li>



<li><strong>For Security Studies</strong><br>The case challenges liberal assumptions that human security and rule of law must precede coercive dominance.</li>
</ol>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Conclusion</strong></h2>



<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">From a hawkish strategic perspective, El Salvador’s internal security strategy is <strong>rational, effective, and historically consistent</strong> with successful state reassertion campaigns. Its primary vulnerability is not moral legitimacy, but <strong>sustainability</strong>. If the state fails to convert coercive control into durable institutional authority, the current success will represent suppression rather than resolution.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">For war college analysis, El Salvador should be studied not as an anomaly, but as a <strong>deliberate return to coercion-first statecraft</strong> in an era of declining tolerance for internal disorder.</p>
