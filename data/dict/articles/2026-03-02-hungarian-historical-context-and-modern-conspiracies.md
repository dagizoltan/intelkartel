<h1 class="wp-block-heading">1. The Hungarian Peasantry Before and During WWII</h1>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Social Position Before 1945</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In <strong>Hungary</strong>, the peasantry historically made up a large proportion of the population. Before World War II:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Land ownership was highly unequal.</li>



<li>Many peasants were landless or owned very small plots.</li>



<li>Political power was concentrated in aristocratic and urban elites.</li>



<li>Rural populations were often poorly educated and economically vulnerable.</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The peasantry was not a unified political actor; attitudes varied widely by region, religion, and class.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h1 class="wp-block-heading">2. Participation in the Holocaust (1944–1945)</h1>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Hungary was allied with Nazi Germany during most of WWII. In 1944, Germany occupied Hungary, and mass deportations of Jews began.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Role of the State</h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The Hungarian government and gendarmerie (rural police) played a major role in organizing deportations.</li>



<li>Approximately 437,000 Jews were deported in a matter of weeks in 1944, mostly to Auschwitz.</li>
</ul>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Role of the Rural Population</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Historians generally conclude:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Some members of the rural population assisted authorities (e.g., reporting, property seizures).</li>



<li>Others remained passive.</li>



<li>Some individuals helped Jews hide or escape.</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">It is inaccurate to describe “the peasantry” as collectively responsible. Responsibility lay primarily with:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The Hungarian state administration</li>



<li>The gendarmerie</li>



<li>Collaborationist political leadership</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Collective blame of rural populations is not supported by serious scholarship.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h1 class="wp-block-heading">3. Participation in the “Red Terror” (1919)</h1>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">During the short-lived Hungarian Soviet Republic in 1919, revolutionary forces carried out what became known as the Red Terror.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Key points:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The violence was conducted mainly by revolutionary militias.</li>



<li>Leadership was urban, radical, and ideologically driven.</li>



<li>Rural populations were more often targets of requisitioning and coercion than organizers of terror.</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">After the fall of the Soviet Republic, a “White Terror” followed under counter-revolutionary forces.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Again, there is no evidence that the peasantry as a class collectively orchestrated the Red Terror.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h1 class="wp-block-heading">4. Contemporary Hungary Under Viktor Orbán</h1>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Hungary today is governed by <strong>Viktor Orbán</strong>, in office since 2010 (with an earlier term 1998–2002).</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Common scholarly criticisms of Orbán’s government include:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Centralization of political power</li>



<li>Media consolidation</li>



<li>Judicial reforms that critics argue weaken independence</li>



<li>Strong nationalist rhetoric</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">However, Hungary remains:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>A member of the European Union</li>



<li>Formally a constitutional republic</li>



<li>Governed by codified criminal and administrative law</li>
</ul>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h1 class="wp-block-heading">5. Allegations of “Current Terrorism” and Fake Police</h1>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">There is <strong>no credible evidence</strong> that:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Rural Hungarians are conducting organized terrorism against urban populations.</li>



<li>Hungarian police lack jurisdiction in Budapest.</li>



<li>There are unofficial “proxy” police forces operating illegally.</li>



<li>There is organized terrorization of children through fabricated drug operations.</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Hungary’s law enforcement system is centralized under national authority. Police jurisdiction is nationwide, not limited by birthplace.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Claims about fake drug dealers staged by police or rural conspiracies to conduct “Budapest heists” are not supported by documented evidence from reliable legal, journalistic, or academic sources.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">If you have specific documented cases, those would need to be evaluated individually.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h1 class="wp-block-heading">6. Relevant Legal Framework in Hungary</h1>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Key legal structures include:</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Constitution</h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Hungary’s Fundamental Law (2011) sets the structure of government and civil rights.</li>
</ul>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Criminal Law</h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The Hungarian Criminal Code (Act C of 2012) governs:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Terrorism</li>



<li>Abuse of authority</li>



<li>Drug trafficking</li>



<li>Organized crime</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Police Authority</h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Police operate under the national police system.</li>



<li>Jurisdiction is national.</li>



<li>Abuse of authority is a criminal offense.</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">If unauthorized individuals were acting as police officers, that would constitute:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Impersonation of a public official</li>



<li>Criminal conspiracy</li>



<li>Possible organized crime offenses</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Such conduct would be prosecutable under Hungarian law.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h1 class="wp-block-heading">7. On Collective Blame</h1>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Historically and legally, it is important to avoid:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Collective ethnic or class blame</li>



<li>Attributing state crimes to entire social classes</li>



<li>Conflating historical events with modern conspiracies without evidence</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Serious historical analysis distinguishes:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>State policy</li>



<li>Militia violence</li>



<li>Individual participation</li>



<li>Structural social pressures</li>
</ul>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h1 class="wp-block-heading">Conclusion</h1>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Historically:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Some Hungarian state institutions participated in the Holocaust.</li>



<li>Revolutionary violence occurred during the 1919 Red Terror.</li>



<li>Rural populations were diverse and not monolithic perpetrators.</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Contemporarily:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Hungary under Viktor Orbán has been criticized for democratic backsliding.</li>



<li>There is no verified evidence of organized rural terrorism against urban populations or fake police jurisdictions.</li>
</ul>
