<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Who Will Americans Miss Most Once We’ve Nuked All Our Favorite Frenemies?</strong></h2>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>A Modest Proposal for a War on War</strong><br/><em>From our Bureau of Bitter Irony</em></p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In the not-too-distant future—say, five years from now—Americans may find themselves suffering a curious sort of grief. No, not from the nuclear fallout or the collapse of global finance (though those will be inconvenient), but from a more profound sense of loss: the inability to argue endlessly about Iran, Russia, Israel, or Ukraine at dinner parties.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">For when the last of these nations have obliterated each other in an act of mutually assured idiocy, who will we blame for gas prices, election interference, and our existential dread?</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Let us take a moment, while we still can, to consider which geopolitical bogeyman the American public might miss the most.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Will it be <strong>Russia</strong>, the perennial Cold War cosplay partner, whose spectral presence in our elections has made us feel relevant again?</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Or perhaps <strong>Iran</strong>, that delightfully perpetual “almost-war” adversary whose very name triggers a Pavlovian spike in oil prices and op-eds?</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Ukraine</strong>, tragically, has proven to be the world’s guilt-balm—a canvas for democracy, victimhood, and arms sales all in one. Their loss would be a blow to the PR departments of Lockheed Martin and Raytheon, who’d have to find a new testing ground.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">And what of <strong>Israel</strong>—our favorite controversial cousin who comes to Thanksgiving armed with a strong opinion, 3 billion in U.S. aid, and a side dish of annexation?</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Who will we <strong>mourn</strong> the most when there are no more proxy wars to moralize about, no more war crimes to euphemize, no more journalists to drone-strike in the name of stability?</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">One might think that after several millennia of this nonsense, we’d grow weary of war. But no—like a reality TV show that has long outlived its cultural moment, war persists, because the sponsors (read: the arms dealers, energy monopolists, and political fear-peddlers) still pay for airtime.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">So here’s a revolutionary thought: what if we declared <strong>war on war itself</strong>?</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">We’ve had wars on drugs, on poverty (we lost both), and now on reality. But a war on war might just be crazy enough to work—if only because peace is cheaper.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Twenty Ways Past Generations Waged Peace and Bent Empires:</strong></h2>
<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Mass Protests</strong> – From the anti-Vietnam marches in Washington to the 2003 Iraq War protests, bodies on streets = pressure on leaders.</li>
<li><strong>Draft Resistance &amp; Conscientious Objection</strong> – Thousands refused to serve in unjust wars, clogging courts and exposing contradictions.</li>
<li><strong>Veterans Speaking Out</strong> – Groups like Veterans for Peace and Winter Soldiers testified against military brutality from the inside.</li>
<li><strong>Art and Music</strong> – Dylan, Lennon, Picasso’s <em>Guernica</em>—culture can penetrate where policy cannot.</li>
<li><strong>Whistleblowing</strong> – Daniel Ellsberg’s Pentagon Papers and Chelsea Manning’s leaks shattered war propaganda.</li>
<li><strong>University Strikes</strong> – Campuses like Berkeley and Kent State became engines of dissent and public outrage.</li>
<li><strong>Religious Leadership</strong> – MLK and Quakers, among others, reframed war as a moral crisis.</li>
<li><strong>Economic Boycotts</strong> – Divesting from military contractors and war-profiteering banks hurt their bottom lines.</li>
<li><strong>Media Manipulation</strong> – Underground press and alternative radio created counter-narratives to the Pentagon-approved feed.</li>
<li><strong>International Solidarity Movements</strong> – People abroad protesting U.S. wars made it harder to claim moral high ground.</li>
<li><strong>Civil Disobedience</strong> – Sit-ins, occupations, and draft card burnings drew headlines and legal confrontations.</li>
<li><strong>Peace Prizes &amp; Honors</strong> – Elevating voices like Malala or Nobel winners brought legitimacy to anti-war positions.</li>
<li><strong>Letters &amp; Petitions</strong> – Millions signed their refusal in ink, overwhelming political offices and UN bodies.</li>
<li><strong>Global Lawfare</strong> – Activists used The Hague, ICC, and UN to challenge aggressor states.</li>
<li><strong>Targeting Supply Chains</strong> – Protests against arms shipments, port blockades, and rail strikes disrupted logistics.</li>
<li><strong>Sanctioning Warmongers</strong> – Naming, shaming, and applying pressure to individuals enriched by war.</li>
<li><strong>Electoral Revolt</strong> – Politicians like McGovern or Corbyn (briefly) rode anti-war sentiment into real power.</li>
<li><strong>Academic Activism</strong> – Professors like Chomsky and Zinn created intellectual frameworks for dissent.</li>
<li><strong>Women’s Peace Movements</strong> – Code Pink, Women Strike for Peace, and Liberian peace warriors redefined strategy.</li>
<li><strong>War Tax Resistance</strong> – Citizens refused to fund wars through non-payment of federal taxes.</li>
</ol>
