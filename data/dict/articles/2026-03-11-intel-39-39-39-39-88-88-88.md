<h1 class="wp-block-heading">Comparative Study: Methods of Political Power Maintenance</h1>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">1. Classic authoritarian regimes (Iraq, Libya, Syria, Iran)</h2>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">These systems were/are <strong>coercive security states</strong> where maintaining power relies heavily on <strong>direct repression</strong>.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Iraq under Saddam Hussein</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Extrajudicial killings:</strong> Widely documented by organizations like Human Rights Watch. Political opponents and suspected dissidents were executed without trial.</li>
<li><strong>Arbitrary arrests:</strong> Massive use of security services (Mukhabarat) to detain critics.</li>
<li><strong>Torture:</strong> Systematic in prisons such as Abu Ghraib under the regime.</li>
<li><strong>Repression of protests:</strong> Uprisings like the <strong>1991 Shiite and Kurdish rebellions</strong> were violently crushed.</li>
<li><strong>Political structure:</strong> Personalist dictatorship with a cult of personality.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Goal:</strong> eliminate all organized opposition through fear.</p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Libya under Muammar Gaddafi</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Extrajudicial killings:</strong> Security forces targeted dissidents at home and abroad.</li>
<li><strong>Arbitrary arrests:</strong> Political prisoners held for long periods without trial.</li>
<li><strong>Torture:</strong> Documented in state prisons.</li>
<li><strong>Repression of protests:</strong> During the First Libyan Civil War (2011), protests were met with lethal force.</li>
<li><strong>Political structure:</strong> Revolutionary authoritarian system with weak institutions and rule by networks loyal to Gaddafi.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Goal:</strong> maintain revolutionary legitimacy and personal rule.</p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Syria under Bashar al‑Assad</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Extrajudicial killings:</strong> Particularly during the Syrian Civil War after 2011.</li>
<li><strong>Arbitrary arrests:</strong> Large-scale detention of suspected opposition activists.</li>
<li><strong>Torture:</strong> Extensively documented in Syrian detention facilities.</li>
<li><strong>Repression of protests:</strong> Initial peaceful protests in 2011 were violently suppressed.</li>
<li><strong>Political structure:</strong> Authoritarian state dominated by security services.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Goal:</strong> survival of regime and ruling coalition.</p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Iran under Ali Khamenei (Islamic Republic leadership)</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Extrajudicial killings:</strong> Allegations in suppression of protests.</li>
<li><strong>Arbitrary arrests:</strong> Activists, journalists, and opposition figures detained.</li>
<li><strong>Torture:</strong> Reported in detention facilities.</li>
<li><strong>Repression of protests:</strong> For example during protests after the 2009 Iranian presidential election protests and later demonstrations.</li>
<li><strong>Political structure:</strong> Hybrid system combining elections with strong clerical oversight.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Goal:</strong> protect ideological system of the Islamic Republic.</p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Venezuela under Nicolás Maduro</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Extrajudicial killings:</strong> Allegations tied to special police units.</li>
<li><strong>Arbitrary arrests:</strong> Opposition politicians and activists detained.</li>
<li><strong>Torture:</strong> Documented cases reported by international organizations.</li>
<li><strong>Repression of protests:</strong> Violent crackdowns during economic crisis protests.</li>
<li><strong>Political structure:</strong> Electoral authoritarian regime.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Goal:</strong> retain power despite economic collapse and political opposition.</p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h1 class="wp-block-heading">2. Common patterns in authoritarian survival</h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Political science research identifies several <strong>recurring tools</strong>:</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">A. Security apparatus control</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Intelligence services</li>
<li>secret police</li>
<li>loyal military units</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">B. Elimination of opposition</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>arrests</li>
<li>intimidation</li>
<li>exile</li>
<li>killings</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">C. Information control</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>propaganda</li>
<li>censorship</li>
<li>media monopolies</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">D. Patronage networks</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>distributing resources to loyal elites</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">E. Fear as a political tool</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>visible punishment of dissent.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">These are characteristic of <strong>hard authoritarian regimes</strong>.</p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h1 class="wp-block-heading">3. Hungary under Viktor Orbán (NER system)</h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Hungary is usually classified by scholars as <strong>“competitive authoritarian” or “hybrid regime”</strong>, not a dictatorship.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Key features identified by political scientists:</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Institutional control</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Changes to constitution and electoral law</li>
<li>Centralization of state institutions</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Media influence</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Large pro-government media ecosystem</li>
<li>Government advertising supporting friendly outlets</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Economic patronage</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Business networks aligned with the ruling party</li>
<li>Redistribution of state contracts</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Electoral advantage</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>District redesign</li>
<li>campaign finance asymmetries</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Pressure on civil society</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Legal or administrative pressure on NGOs</li>
<li>rhetoric against critical institutions.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">These mechanisms are mostly <strong>legal-institutional rather than violent repression</strong>.</p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h1 class="wp-block-heading">4. Key differences between the regimes</h1>

<h1 class="wp-block-heading">5. Political science insight</h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Research on authoritarian regimes suggests <strong>two broad survival models</strong>:</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Coercion model</h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Used by regimes like:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Saddam Hussein</li>
<li>Bashar al-Assad</li>
<li>Muammar Gaddafi</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Power is maintained mainly by <strong>violence and fear</strong>.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Institutionalized hybrid model</h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Used by regimes like:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Viktor Orbán</li>
<li>Nicolás Maduro</li>
<li>some other modern populist governments</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Power is maintained through:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>control of institutions</li>
<li>legal changes</li>
<li>media influence</li>
<li>economic networks</li>
<li>electoral advantage.</li>
</ul>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h1 class="wp-block-heading">6. Why regimes collapse</h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Examples:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Saddam Hussein → removed after the 2003 invasion of Iraq</li>
<li>Gaddafi → overthrown during the First Libyan Civil War</li>
<li>Assad → still in power due to military support and allies</li>
<li>Maduro → remains due to military and institutional control.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Collapse usually occurs when <strong>three pillars break</strong>:</p>
<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li>elite loyalty</li>
<li>military loyalty</li>
<li>economic capacity.</li>
</ol>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">✅ <strong>Key takeaway</strong></p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">While there are some <strong>broad similarities in how governments consolidate power</strong>, the regimes you listed operate at <strong>very different levels of repression</strong>.</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Iraq, Libya, and Syria relied heavily on <strong>violent coercion</strong>.</li>
<li>Iran mixes repression with ideological institutions.</li>
<li>Venezuela and Hungary rely more on <strong>institutional control and political advantage</strong>, though critics debate the degree of democratic backsliding.</li>
</ul>
