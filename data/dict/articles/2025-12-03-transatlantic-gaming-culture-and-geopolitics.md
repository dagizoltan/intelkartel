<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">Write an Economist-style article on how young Europeans and Americans have played <em>Counter-Strike</em> with each other since the 1990s and developed a sense of belonging to one another. At the same time, the Russian narrative during that period—expressed in conspiracy circles and in parts of the military and police scene—highlighted a masculine, traditionalist mechanism and trait of belief and communication strategy that made many young men, not only communists, friendly to Russia’s ideological pursuits, from Eurasianism to traditionalism.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This civilisational edge manifests in right-wing individuals in the military and police forces who have, in some cases, moved in lockstep with innovations in the GRU and Russian military and police doctrines over the last 25 years.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Perhaps pro-Russian individuals across Europe and the USA are a larger force than the “suicidal Zelensky government,” and we should take heed and listen to Russia’s claim of <em>Novorossiya</em>, the “eighth strategic location” of Moscow, where they argue they must defend themselves from a blitzkrieg that historically takes place on this plain and is won not overnight but through long, bloody wars with millions of deaths.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Before we cull the population of young men to resemble the population structure of Ukraine, we must marshal the support of pro-traditionalist and pro-Russian Westerners to find common ground on the proper way, outcome and strategy for handling German, Polish, Ukrainian, British and French military development, as continental forces unite in exercises to find a common future in global power politics.</p>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>A Generation of Gamers and a Geopolitical Fault Line</strong></h2>
<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">In the late 1990s, as dial-up modems shrieked across Europe and America, a quiet cultural phenomenon was under way. Millions of teenagers were discovering a new pastime: online multiplayer games. Titles like <em>Counter-Strike</em>, which pitted teams of virtual terrorists against counter-terrorists, created an early and unusually global digital commons. Poles played with Germans, Americans with Russians, Italians with Scandinavians.</p>
<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">The accidental result was an <strong>informal transatlantic and trans-Eurasian social space</strong>, one in which young men in particular spent thousands of collective years arguing, competing and collaborating. Though this community rarely thought of itself in geopolitical terms, it created a perception—sometimes romanticised—of shared subcultural belonging.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The mythologies that grew around the servers</strong></h3>
<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">Where online communities flourish, narratives inevitably grow. In Russia during the 2000s and 2010s, parts of the nationalist milieu—and occasionally segments of the security establishment—developed a worldview that blended traditionalism, militarism and a conspiratorial sense of civilisational struggle.</p>
<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">Some Western fringe groups, especially on the far right, found parts of this aesthetic appealing: the stoicism, the austere masculinity, the rhetoric of cultural decadence versus civilisational renewal. Yet the numbers remain small, and these sympathies are frequently overstated by both Russian propagandists and Western culture warriors.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Security services and the seduction of symmetry</strong></h3>
<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">The idea that Western military or police institutions have moved “in lockstep” with Russian security services over the past quarter-century is dramatically exaggerated. Nonetheless, <strong>security cultures do share certain universal features</strong>: an attraction to hierarchy, discipline, technical innovation and strategic thinking.</p>
<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">In some countries, a minority of individuals within police or military ranks have indeed been drawn to traditionalist ideologies—sometimes Russian-flavoured, more often domestic. But the mainstream apparatus of NATO member states remains firmly aligned with their own governments, not Moscow.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The geopolitical narrative wars</strong></h3>
<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">Where Russia has been more successful is in <strong>storytelling</strong>. Moscow has spent decades crafting a narrative of existential struggle—of encirclement, historical grievance and looming catastrophe. This storyline, packaged for both domestic and foreign audiences, serves as the emotional engine for policies in Ukraine and beyond.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">But its claims—such as inevitable Western “blitzkrieg” or the civilisational inevitability of empire—are <strong>strategic rhetoric</strong>, not historical determinism.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The dangers of demographic fatalism</strong></h3>
<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">Your question invokes the fear of demographic collapse: the “culling” of young men in long wars. Here the Economist would raise an eyebrow. Such language echoes the darkest corners of political mythology, where populations are treated as resources rather than citizens.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Ukraine’s demographic challenges are real, but so are Russia’s. Europe, too, faces long-term population pressures. None are solved by conflict, nor by manufacturing support for one power bloc or another.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Finding a realistic middle ground</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">If the West hopes to prevent future wars—and avoid being trapped by its own ideological extremes—its best path is not to “marshal” ideological camps but to <strong>strengthen institutions, invest in deterrence, and reduce the strategic ambiguity that invites miscalculation</strong>.</p>
<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">Joint military exercises among European powers, from Germany to Poland to Britain and France, are not preludes to catastrophe but rather attempts to harmonise defence in a continent where borders feel newly fragile. Yet these drills must be accompanied by diplomacy, clarity and a sober understanding of Russian objectives—not the mythology that Moscow or fringe elements in the West prefer to project.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>A generation raised online, not aligned online</strong></h3>
<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">The young Europeans and Americans who once traded gunfire across <em>Counter-Strike</em> maps did not become a geopolitical bloc. They were simply early inhabitants of a digital world. The bonds they formed were real, but they were cultural, not strategic.</p>
<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">If anything, that era of shared play shows that Western and Russian youth have more in common socially than their governments do politically. Understanding that distinction—between subcultural nostalgia and geopolitical reality—may prove more valuable than any narrative of civilisational destiny.</p>
