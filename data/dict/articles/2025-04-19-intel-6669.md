<h2 class="wp-block-heading">🇱🇹 <strong>Lithuania (1990s–2004): Criminals to Capitalists</strong></h2>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">🕵️‍♂️ <strong>The Situation</strong></h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>In the 1990s, <strong>Lithuania had rampant organized crime</strong>, smuggling (especially cigarettes and fuel), racketeering, and links between business and crime.</li>
<li>Criminal groups began <strong>legitimizing</strong> by investing in real estate, transport, banking, and construction.</li>
<li>These were <em>“grey capitalists”</em>—not entirely clean, but shifting to the legal economy.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">🔄 <strong>How It Shifted During EU Accession</strong></h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>EU integration forced <strong>legal and regulatory reforms</strong> that made the criminal environment harder to operate in.</li>
<li>Many criminal actors <strong>morphed into businesspeople</strong>, using early wealth to dominate privatization or legitimate enterprise.</li>
<li>State institutions became stronger; law enforcement and tax scrutiny improved.</li>
<li>Result: <strong>Criminality didn’t disappear</strong>, but it <strong>evolved</strong> into oligarch-lite or business elites, mostly without open defiance of state power.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">✅ What Helped Lithuania:</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Small size → easier control.</li>
<li>Politically united pro-EU vision.</li>
<li>Less geopolitical pressure (not in active war like Ukraine).</li>
<li>EU entry was framed as <em>existential</em>, not optional.</li>
</ul>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">🇺🇦 <strong>Ukraine: The Modern Struggle</strong></h2>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">🧨 <strong>The Situation</strong></h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Ukraine’s post-Soviet elites never left the shadows—they became <strong>oligarchs</strong> who <strong>captured the state</strong>, economy, and media.</li>
<li>Unlike Lithuania, criminal-political-business networks became <strong>entrenched and institutionalized</strong>, not cleaned out.</li>
<li>Even during the war, some elites are accused of profiteering, dodging reforms, or playing both sides.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">🔐 <strong>EU Benchmarks for Ukraine</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Ukraine must hit similar but more intense criteria, including:</p>
<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Judicial independence</strong> — dismantling control by oligarchs and the President’s office.</li>
<li><strong>Anti-corruption</strong> — empower NABU, SAPO, and anti-graft courts <em>without interference</em>.</li>
<li><strong>De-oligarchization</strong> — reduce the dominance of tycoons in media, politics, and strategic industries.</li>
<li><strong>Security services reform</strong> — especially the SBU, which still has KGB-style legacy powers.</li>
<li><strong>Public procurement &amp; transparency</strong> — to meet EU fiscal standards.</li>
<li><strong>War-time complications</strong> — managing reforms <em>while fighting a war</em> is unprecedented in EU history.</li>
</ol>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">🧠 Bottom Line</h2>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Lithuania successfully shifted many criminal interests into the formal economy</strong>, helped by small scale, political will, and EU carrots.</li>
<li><strong>Ukraine’s task is harder</strong>: it must <strong>dismantle elite structures</strong> that have ruled for 30 years—<strong>during a war</strong>—while rebuilding a legal system that the same elites hollowed out.</li>
</ul>
