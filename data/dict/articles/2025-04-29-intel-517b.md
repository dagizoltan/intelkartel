<h1 class="wp-block-heading">Political History and Governance</h1>

<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Since the end of communism Hungary has held regular multi-party elections. The first free parliamentary election (1990) returned an <strong>MDF‚ÄìFKGP‚ÄìKDNP</strong> coalition with J√≥zsef Antall (MDF) as PM‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_prime_ministers_of_Hungary#:~:text=53%20%20Image%20J%C3%B3zsef%20Antall,Boross%20MDF%20%E2%80%93%20370%E2%80%93KDNP" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>. Subsequent elections produced alternating governments: MSZP-led from 1994 (Gyula Horn) to 1998, then Fidesz-led (Viktor Orb√°n) 1998‚Äì2002, an MSZP-led government 2002‚Äì2010, and <strong>Fidesz‚ÄìKDNP</strong> governments from 2010 onward (Orb√°n re-elected in 2014, 2018, 2022). In 2010 Fidesz‚ÄìKDNP won 263 of 386 seats (68%)‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2010_Hungarian_parliamentary_election#:~:text=263%20%2F%20386" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>; it preserved two-thirds majorities in 2014, 2018 and 2022 (e.g. 133/199 seats in 2018‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2018_Hungarian_parliamentary_election#:~:text=Seats%C2%A0won" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>). Viktor Orb√°n served as PM in 1998‚Äì2002 and again from 2010‚Äìpresent‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_prime_ministers_of_Hungary#:~:text=56%20%20Image%20Viktor%20Orb%C3%A1n,93%20%20Orb%C3%A1n%20I" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_prime_ministers_of_Hungary#:~:text=,2022%20%20Incumbent%20%2086" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Hungary‚Äôs constitution was replaced in 2011‚Äì12. On 18 April 2011 Parliament approved the new ‚ÄúFundamental Law‚Äù (promulgated by the President and effective 1 Jan 2012) ‚Äì the first wholly new Hungarian constitution since 1949‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Hungary#:~:text=The%20Fundamental%20Law%20of%20Hungary,framework%20and%20following%20free%20elections" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>. The new charter and its many amendments have been controversial (opponents cite weakened checks and a conservative ideological bent)‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Hungary#:~:text=The%20Fundamental%20Law%20of%20Hungary,framework%20and%20following%20free%20elections" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Hungary#:~:text=In%20March%202013%2C%20Parliament%20amended,power%20to%20review%20the%20constitution" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>. For example, a major 2013 amendment (passed by the Fidesz supermajority) limited the Constitutional Court‚Äôs powers (prohibiting annulment of two-thirds laws) and introduced new judicial retirement rules‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Hungary#:~:text=In%20March%202013%2C%20Parliament%20amended,power%20to%20review%20the%20constitution" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>. (Orb√°n‚Äôs government defended these changes as necessary to clarify the new constitution‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitution_of_Hungary#:~:text=In%20March%202013%2C%20Parliament%20amended,power%20to%20review%20the%20constitution" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>.)</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Parliament remains unicameral (the <strong>Orsz√°ggy≈±l√©s</strong>), and by custom executive power is with the PM. Key political events include EU accession (May 2004) and joining NATO (March 1999), reflecting Hungary‚Äôs post-1989 Westward orientation. (Recent foreign policy has also pursued ties with Eastern countries and significant international investment.)</p>

<h1 class="wp-block-heading">Economic Data</h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Hungary‚Äôs economy underwent rapid privatization in the 1990s, EU accession in 2004, and macroeconomic shocks in 2009 (GDP ‚Äì6.7%) and 2020 (COVID contraction). By 2010‚Äì2019 growth averaged roughly 3‚Äì4%/yr, driven by industrial exports (autos, electronics) and EU funds. Inflation was low (single-digit) until 2021, when energy-price shocks pushed it into double digits. In recent years government policies (sector taxes, wage increases) and global conditions have affected inflation and growth.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">According to Eurostat, Hungary‚Äôs GDP per capita (PPS) by 2018 was about <strong>66% of the EU27 average</strong>‚Äã<a href="https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/documents/12743486/14207633/HU-EN.pdf#:~:text=107%20per%20km2%20Household%20disposable,2018%29%2065" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">ec.europa.eu</a>, reflecting continued catch‚Äëup. The economy is export-oriented (exports ~80% of GDP, mainly to Germany, Austria and EU partners) and has attracted substantial foreign direct investment (especially in automotive and tech sectors). Agriculture is a small share (&lt;4% GDP), industry ~25%, services ~70%.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Key macro indicators (approximate): GDP growth averaged +3% in the 2010s; inflation ~3‚Äì4% (pre-2022); unemployment ~3‚Äì4% in late 2010s, rising slightly during crises. Public debt has hovered around 70% of GDP. <em>(Detailed time-series data on GDP, inflation, unemployment, trade balance, investment etc. are available from Hungarian sources (KSH), Eurostat and IMF.)</em></p>
<h1 class="wp-block-heading">Demographics</h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Hungary‚Äôs population has declined since 1990. Census and estimates show roughly <strong>10.4 million</strong> in 1990, falling to <strong>10.2M</strong> (2001), <strong>9.94M</strong> (2011) and <strong>9.60M</strong> (2022)‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_Hungary#:~:text=1990%2010%2C374%2C823%E2%88%920.32,2022%209%2C603%2C634%E2%88%920.31" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>. (Eurostat estimates put January 2019 at 9.8M‚Äã<a href="https://ec.europa.eu/eurostat/documents/12743486/14207633/HU-EN.pdf#:~:text=Population%20%281,2018" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">ec.europa.eu</a>, and 2025 at ~9.66M‚Äã<a href="https://datareportal.com/reports/digital-2025-hungary#:~:text=Population%20of%20Hungary%20in%202025" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">datareportal.com</a>.) Population density is ~105 people/km¬≤. The population is aging: low birth-rates and rising life expectancy (around <strong>76 years</strong> in 2022‚Äã<a href="https://www.macrotrends.net/global-metrics/countries/HUN/hungary/life-expectancy#:~:text=,increase%20from%202021" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">macrotrends.net</a>) mean a high median age (~43) and increasing old-age dependency (over 30%). Urbanization is high (~73% urban‚Äã<a href="https://datareportal.com/reports/digital-2025-hungary#:~:text=The%20population%20of%20Hungary%20stood,66%20million%20in%20January%C2%A02025" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">datareportal.com</a>), with Budapest the largest city (~1.7M metro). Net migration was negative in the 1990s‚Äì2000s, but in the 2010s EU labor mobility partly offset natural decline.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Ethnic composition (2011 census):</strong> about <strong>93.5% Hungarian</strong> (Magyar)‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_Hungary#:~:text=ethnically%20homogeneous%20after%20World%20War,10" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>. Largest minorities include Roma (~3.2%), Germans (~1.9%), and Romanians and Slovaks (each ~0.4%)‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_Hungary#:~:text=County%20%20Hungarian%20%20Bulgarian,0.0" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>. Other ethnicities (Croat, Serb, Slovene, Ukrainian, Romanian, etc.) each comprise much smaller shares (&lt;0.5%). The Hungarian language is the official mother tongue; ‚Äúmore than nine-tenths of the population is ethnically Hungarian and speaks Hungarian‚Äù‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_Hungary#:~:text=ethnically%20homogeneous%20after%20World%20War,10" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>. Several minority languages (e.g. Roma, German, Slovak) have cultural recognition.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Religion (2022 census):</strong> Hungary is traditionally Christian. By 2022 about <strong>30%</strong> of the population identified as Catholic (mostly Roman Catholic, 28.3%; Greek Catholic 1.7%), <strong>11.6%</strong> as Protestant (9.8% Calvinist, 1.8% Lutheran), and <strong>0.2%</strong> Orthodox Christian‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_Hungary#:~:text=Religious%20affiliation%20in%20Hungary%20%282022%29,2" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>. Jews comprise ~0.1%. Other religions (e.g. Islam, Eastern faiths, folk) total ~1.8%. A large share ‚Äì <strong>16.2%</strong> ‚Äì reported no religious affiliation, and <strong>40.1%</strong> did not answer this census question‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_Hungary#:~:text=Religious%20affiliation%20in%20Hungary%20%282022%29,2" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_Hungary#:~:text=Total%20religions%204%2C201%2C491%2043,00" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>. (By comparison, the 2011 census had shown ~52.9% Christian, ~27% non-religious/atheist, ~18% undeclared.)</p>

<h1 class="wp-block-heading">Cultural Developments</h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Hungarian is the official language (a Uralic language unrelated to most neighbors) and is spoken by virtually all native Hungarians‚Äã<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_Hungary#:~:text=ethnically%20homogeneous%20after%20World%20War,10" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">en.wikipedia.org</a>. Minority communities often also use their heritage languages (e.g. German in Baranya/Budapest, Slovak, Romani, etc.). <p class="wp-block-paragraph">Major national cultural events include the Budapest Spring Festival (arts), Sziget Festival (music), and numerous folk traditions. <h1 class="wp-block-heading">Technology</h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Hungary has high digital connectivity. By early 2025 there were about <strong>9.09 million Internet users</strong> in a population of ~9.66M, i.e. <strong>94.1% penetration</strong>‚Äã<a href="https://datareportal.com/reports/digital-2025-hungary#:~:text=services%20such%20as%20voice%20and,include%20access%20to%20the%20internet" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">datareportal.com</a>. Broadband internet access is widespread in cities and growing in rural areas. ICT investment has been strong: R&amp;D spending has risen (though still around 1.4‚Äì1.6% of GDP, below EU average). Hungary has a growing tech startup scene (particularly in Budapest), supported by EU funds and domestic innovation grants. High-tech exports (pharmaceuticals, software, electronics) account for a significant share of industrial output.</p>
<h1 class="wp-block-heading">Environment</h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Hungary has a <strong>temperate continental climate</strong>: hot summers, cold winters. Environmental issues include air pollution (industrial and vehicle emissions in urban centers), and management of the Danube-Tisza watershed. As an EU member, Hungary adheres to EU climate targets and seeks to increase renewable energy. <strong>Energy</strong>: Hungary‚Äôs electricity mix is ~50% nuclear (Paks plant), ~30% gas/coal, ~10% renewables (wind, solar, biomass) and the rest imports. The government aims for &gt;20% renewables by 2030. Per capita CO‚ÇÇ emissions have been around 6‚Äì7 tonnes/year. Air quality data (PM10, PM2.5) show improvements in recent decades but some exceedances in winter. Hungary participates in international environmental agreements (Kyoto, Paris Climate, biodiversity conventions).</p>
<h1 class="wp-block-heading">Military</h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Hungary joined <strong>NATO</strong> in March 1999 and the EU in May 2004, solidifying its post‚ÄëCold War alignment. The Hungarian Defence Forces are modest in size (roughly <strong>23,000 active personnel</strong> around 2020, plus reserves). Defense spending has been rising: it was near the NATO guideline of 2% of GDP by the early 2020s. Hungary contributes forces to NATO and EU missions (e.g. KFOR in Kosovo, UN peacekeeping). Major military equipment includes Leopard tanks, Gripen fighters, and domestically produced small arms. (Defence procurement and budgeting are set by the Ministry of Defence; recent years have seen modernization programs for air defense and cyber.)</p>
<h1 class="wp-block-heading">Education</h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Hungary has a highly educated population. <strong>Literacy</strong> is virtually 100%. Primary and secondary education is universal (compulsory through age 16). Tertiary enrollment is high: over 50% of the 25‚Äì34 age cohort holds a higher education degree (above the EU average). Key reforms include the 2011-centralization of all schools under state authority (abolishing local governance) and the 2012 ‚ÄúCampus Hungary‚Äù program to internationalize universities. International PISA scores (OECD tests) for Hungarian students are slightly below the OECD average but improving. Government education spending is about 4‚Äì5% of GDP.</p>
<h1 class="wp-block-heading">Healthcare</h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Hungary has a universal health care system (National Health Insurance Fund), financed by payroll taxes and government subsidy (total health expenditure ~7‚Äì8% of GDP). Life expectancy at birth has risen from ~70 (1990) to about <strong>76.0 years</strong> by 2022‚Äã<a href="https://www.macrotrends.net/global-metrics/countries/HUN/hungary/life-expectancy#:~:text=,increase%20from%202021" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">macrotrends.net</a> (men ~72, women ~79). Infant mortality has fallen to around 4 per 1000 births. Leading health issues include cardiovascular disease (highest mortality in the EU), cancer, and diabetes. Preventive care and healthy lifestyles are policy focuses (e.g. <strong>‚Äúpublic health product tax‚Äù</strong> on high-sugar/fat foods since 2011). Major reforms in the 2000s (e.g. creating a single-payer fund in 2007) have been controversial, but coverage remains high and vaccination rates (e.g. 99% for measles) are among the EU‚Äôs best.</p>
<h1 class="wp-block-heading">International Relations</h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Hungary joined NATO (1999) and the EU (2004) and is part of the Schengen Area (since 2007). It is a member of the UN, OECD (since 1996), OECD, World Trade Organization (1995) and regional bodies like the Visegr√°d Group. Hungarian foreign policy is officially Atlanticist, and Hungary participates in EU and NATO common policies. Since 2010, government policy has emphasized national sovereignty within the EU and has cultivated closer ties with non-Western partners (e.g. China‚Äôs Belt and Road, Russia) while maintaining alliance commitments. Internationally, Hungary contributes to EU integration (e.g. euro area, Schengen) and advocates for regional concerns (e.g. supporting Hungary‚Äôs large diaspora in neighboring states).</p>
