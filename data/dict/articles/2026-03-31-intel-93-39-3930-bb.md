<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>The Invisible Handcuffs</strong><br><em>When the market for crime behaves like a clearance sale</em></p>



<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">In the more excitable corners of public debate, crime and migration are often described in apocalyptic terms: waves, floods, surges—natural disasters with passports. Yet economics, that dismal but clarifying discipline, suggests something more mundane. Strip away the rhetoric and one finds not an invasion, but a market. And like many markets, this one may be suffering from a rather awkward imbalance.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Call it the <strong>crime economy</strong>.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In classical theory, markets tend toward equilibrium. Supply meets demand, prices settle, and everyone—law-abiding or otherwise—adjusts accordingly. But what happens when the supply of crime persistently outpaces demand?</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The answer, if one follows the logic to its absurd conclusion, is simple: the <strong>price of crime collapses</strong>.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">A Glut of Mischief</h3>



<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">Western nations today exhibit a peculiar contradiction. Political discourse insists that crime is both rampant and intolerable. Yet actual demand—for stolen goods, illicit services, or even low-level disorder—has not kept pace with the narrative.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Consumers, after all, can only buy so many stolen bicycles.</p>



<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">Meanwhile, the <em>supply side</em>—a mix of opportunists, organized actors, and the occasionally desperate—remains robust. The result is a classic oversupply problem. Too many would-be offenders chasing too little profitable wrongdoing.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In any other market, this would trigger price competition. And indeed, one might imagine it already has:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Petty theft yields diminishing returns</li>



<li>Illicit trades become saturated</li>



<li>Risk-adjusted “profits” shrink under heavier policing and surveillance</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In short, crime—once a high-risk, high-reward enterprise—is increasingly looking like a bad investment.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Sub-Zero Pricing</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Push the model further and the satire writes itself: when supply vastly exceeds demand, prices can go negative.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Economists saw this during the oil glut of 2020, when producers briefly paid buyers to take crude off their hands. One wonders whether a similarly perverse dynamic could exist in the shadow economy.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Imagine it:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Offenders taking greater risks for smaller gains</li>



<li>Criminal networks expanding simply to maintain relevance</li>



<li>The “value” of illicit activity dropping below the cost of participation</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">At that point, crime ceases to be rational enterprise and becomes something closer to noise—activity for its own sake, untethered from economic logic.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Migration and Misdiagnosis</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Into this already confused marketplace enters migration, frequently blamed as both cause and multiplier. Yet this too is often treated less as a measurable phenomenon and more as a narrative device.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Labour markets, not crime markets, are where migration has its clearest effects. New arrivals tend to compete for jobs, not for opportunities in burglary. The idea that there exists a unified, ever-expanding “supply of crime” tied neatly to migration is, at best, an oversimplification.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">At worst, it is a category error.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Crime is not a commodity imported in bulk. It is a behaviour shaped by incentives, institutions, and opportunities—most of which are domestic.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">The Theatre of Scarcity</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Why, then, does the perception of crisis persist?</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Because politics thrives not on equilibrium, but on imbalance. A stable system—where crime is managed, migration is absorbed, and markets function imperfectly but predictably—is narratively dull.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Crisis, by contrast, is vivid.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">And so the public is presented with a paradoxical spectacle: a supposed explosion of crime in a system where, by many measures, the “returns” to crime are steadily diminishing.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">It is as if the market were flooded with suppliers shouting ever louder, even as customers quietly exit the shop.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">An Unprofitable Future</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">If one were to take the economic metaphor seriously, the long-term outlook is almost comical.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">A saturated crime market, declining returns, and rising enforcement costs point toward a future where:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The incentives for crime weaken</li>



<li>The risks remain stubbornly high</li>



<li>The entire enterprise becomes, in economic terms, inefficient</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Not a moral victory, perhaps—but a market correction.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Final Thought</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The notion that Western societies are overwhelmed by an ever-growing, ever-profitable “industry of crime” may say more about perception than reality.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Markets, even shadowy ones, obey certain rules.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">And if those rules hold, then the greatest threat to crime may not be policy, policing, or politics—</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">but simple, relentless <strong>oversupply</strong>.</p>
