<h2 class="wp-block-heading">DOCUMENTARY TRANSCRIPT</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><em>“EVERYTHING HAS EARS”</em></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>An Investigative Report on Surveillance, Memory, and Power in Post-Soviet Europe</strong></p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">OPENING SCENE</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Wide shot:</strong> Budapest at night. Apartment windows lit. Trams passing.<br><strong>Ambient sound:</strong> low electrical hum, distant traffic.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>NARRATOR (V.O.)</strong><br>In Hungary, there is a saying: <em>“Mindennek füle van.”</em><br>Everything has ears.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">It’s not a joke. It’s a warning.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">PART I — WHAT WE KNOW</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Archival footage:</strong> Cold War diagrams, telephone exchanges, concrete housing blocks.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>NARRATOR (V.O.)</strong><br>What <em>is</em> documented is this:<br>During the Cold War, Soviet and Warsaw Pact states built extensive internal surveillance systems. These included wiretaps, signal interception, and monitoring facilities embedded in civilian infrastructure—hotels, ministries, telecom exchanges.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This was not secret. It was policy.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">When the Soviet Union collapsed in 1991, those systems did not disappear overnight.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">They were inherited.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">INTERVIEW — FORMER TELECOM ENGINEER (FACE OBSCURED)</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>ENGINEER</strong><br>“We received buildings with rooms we were told not to open. No paperwork. No keys. Sometimes just sealed doors. After privatization, ownership changed—but nobody knew who owned <em>those</em> rooms.”</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">PART II — THE PRIVATIZATION GAP</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Graphics:</strong> Timeline 1991–1998.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>NARRATOR (V.O.)</strong><br>Across Central and Eastern Europe, privatization happened fast. Too fast for audits. Too fast for inventories.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Telecommunications, utilities, property portfolios—sold, split, resold.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Oversight fractured.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Documentation vanished.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">INTERVIEW — ECONOMIC HISTORIAN</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>HISTORIAN</strong><br>“The assumption was political: the system is gone, therefore the surveillance is gone. But infrastructure doesn’t collapse the way ideology does.”</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">PART III — ORGANIZED CRIME ENTERS THE VACUUM</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Archival footage:</strong> 1990s border crossings, police seizures, newspaper headlines.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>NARRATOR (V.O.)</strong><br>At the same time, new networks expanded across the region.<br>Heroin routes from Afghanistan, established during decades of war, moved through the Balkans and Central Europe.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Organized crime needed three things:</p>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>secure communications</li>



<li>advance warning</li>



<li>silence</li>
</ul>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Former state specialists—engineers, technicians, signal experts—were suddenly unemployed.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Some went private.<br>Some went criminal.<br>Some disappeared from records entirely.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">INTERVIEW — FORMER LAW ENFORCEMENT OFFICIAL</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>OFFICIAL</strong><br>“We kept losing cases. Raids anticipated. Phones quiet. Someone always knew. We blamed corruption. But sometimes corruption isn’t bribery—it’s information.”</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">PART IV — THE CLAIMS</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>NARRATOR (V.O.)</strong><br>Here is where facts end—and allegations begin.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">There is <strong>no verified evidence</strong> of continent-wide covert listening systems currently operated by narco-terrorist networks.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">There is <strong>no proof</strong> that unexplained illnesses known as “Havana Syndrome” are caused by leftover or repurposed Cold War technology.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">But there <em>are</em> unanswered questions.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">INTERVIEW — SECURITY ANALYST</h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>ANALYST</strong><br>“When infrastructure is opaque, people fill the gaps. Trauma plus secrecy equals narrative. Some of those narratives are wrong. Some might not be.”</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">PART V — “HAVANA SYNDROME” AND FEAR</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>NARRATOR (V.O.)</strong><br>“Havana Syndrome” remains medically and scientifically contested.<br>Causes proposed include environmental factors, stress, psychosomatic effects, and unknown mechanisms.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">What matters here is not the diagnosis—but the reaction.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In societies with a lived memory of total surveillance, unexplained symptoms are rarely interpreted as accidents.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">They are interpreted as signals.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">PART VI — HUNGARY TODAY</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Street interviews:</strong> ordinary people, faces blurred.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>CITIZEN 1</strong><br>“I don’t think the government listens to everyone. I think <em>someone</em> listens. Always someone.”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>CITIZEN 2</strong><br>“My parents lived through informants. You don’t forget that. You just stop talking.”</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">PART VII — THE CORE QUESTION</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>NARRATOR (V.O.)</strong><br>So the question is not:</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><em>Is everything listening?</em></p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The real question is:</p>



<blockquote class="wp-block-quote is-layout-flow wp-block-quote-is-layout-flow">
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Who controls the systems we never dismantled?</strong><br><strong>Who benefits from silence?</strong><br><strong>And what happens when no one audits the past?</strong></p>
</blockquote>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">FINAL SCENE</h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Shot:</strong> Old telephone exchange being demolished.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>NARRATOR (V.O.)</strong><br>Governments fell. Borders shifted.<br>But wires last longer than regimes.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">And in places where no one ever explained what was removed—<br>people assume something remained.</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Because in the absence of truth,<br><strong>everything has ears.</strong></p>
