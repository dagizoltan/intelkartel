<h1 class="wp-block-heading">🇭🇺 <strong>LONG-FORM COMPARISON</strong></h1>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Duties &amp; Responsibilities of Hungarian Citizens</h2>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Fundamental Law of Hungary (2011) vs. Constitution of 1949 (as amended to 2011)</h3>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h1 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>I. Introduction</strong></h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Hungary has had two major constitutional frameworks since World War II:</p>
<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Act XX of 1949</strong>, which—although heavily amended after 1989—remained the formal constitution until 2011.</li>
<li>The <strong>Fundamental Law of Hungary</strong>, adopted in 2011 and effective since 1 January 2012.</li>
</ol>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Both documents define <em>rights</em> and <em>duties</em>.<br/>Here we examine <strong>citizen duties</strong>, meaning obligations imposed on individuals toward the community, state, or society.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The difference between the two documents reflects:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>the ideological shift from a socialist state to a liberal-democratic system (after 1989),</li>
<li>and later to a national-conservative constitutional identity (2011).</li>
</ul>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h1 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>II. Duties Under the CURRENT Fundamental Law (2011)</strong></h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The Fundamental Law uses the term <strong>“responsibilities” (kötelezettségek)</strong> more often than “duties.” It frames obligations in a modern, rights-based framework but still assigns binding duties.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>1. Duty to Defend the Country</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Article XXXI</strong></p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Key elements:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>All Hungarian citizens</strong> are obliged to <strong>defend Hungary</strong>.</li>
<li>This includes:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>performing <strong>military service</strong> during a state of national crisis;</li>
<li>performing <strong>unarmed service</strong> if military service conflicts with conscience;</li>
<li>participating in <strong>reserve service</strong> under certain conditions.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The duty can become active only in specific legal states (e.g., state of danger, war).</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The Fundamental Law ties defense to:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>national sovereignty</strong>,</li>
<li><strong>territorial integrity</strong>,</li>
<li>and <strong>constitutional order</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>2. Special Obligations in Times of Crisis</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Also in <strong>Article XXXI</strong>:</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">During:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>a <strong>state of national crisis</strong>,</li>
<li>or when <strong>Parliament introduces preventive defense measures</strong>,</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">citizens may be required to:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>perform <strong>defense-related work</strong>,</li>
<li>provide <strong>economic and material services</strong>,</li>
<li>cooperate in <strong>civil defense</strong> (evacuation, emergency tasks, disaster management).</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">These obligations are regulated by <strong>“cardinal Acts”</strong> (laws requiring 2/3 majority).</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>3. Duty to Contribute to Public Needs (Taxes, Social Contributions)</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Article XXX</strong></p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This article expresses social solidarity:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Everyone must contribute to <strong>public needs</strong> according to:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><em>their abilities</em></li>
<li><em>their participation in the economy</em></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This includes:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>taxes,</li>
<li>social security contributions,</li>
<li>fees and duties.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">An important new concept (not found in the 1949 Constitution):</p>
<blockquote class="wp-block-quote is-layout-flow wp-block-quote-is-layout-flow">
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>“When assessing the extent of the contribution, child-rearing must be taken into account.”</strong></p>
</blockquote>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This reflects a demographic family-support principle of the Fundamental Law.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>4. Responsibility for Future Generations – Environmental and Cultural Duties</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Article P</strong></p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This is one of the most distinctive duties in the new constitutional order.</p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Citizens (and the state) must protect:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>natural resources,</li>
<li>agricultural land,</li>
<li>forests and water resources,</li>
<li>biodiversity,</li>
<li>cultural heritage,</li>
<li>and “common national assets.”</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The article frames <strong>environmental stewardship</strong> as a <em>shared constitutional duty</em>, a modern concept influenced by sustainability principles.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>5. General Social Responsibility</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Article N</strong> states that:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>every person “shall be responsible for himself or herself,”</li>
<li>and must contribute to the performance of community tasks <strong>to the best of their abilities and potential</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This is a broad moral/constitutional expectation:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>self-reliance,</li>
<li>contribution to society,</li>
<li>personal responsibility.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Though general, it is enforceable through underlying legislation (e.g., social security, public obligations).</p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h1 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>III. Duties Under the PREVIOUS Constitution (Act XX of 1949)</strong></h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Although originally Soviet-style, the 1989 constitutional reforms made the 1949 Constitution democratic. Still, its structure differed sharply from the 2011 Fundamental Law.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>1. Duty to Defend the Country</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Article 70/H</strong></p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Similar to today, it required:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>defending the country,</li>
<li>performing military service.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Mandatory conscription existed (until it was suspended in 2004).<br/>Conscientious objectors could perform civil service.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>2. Obligations in Time of Emergency</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This article allowed the state to require:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>military work,</li>
<li>economic or material services,</li>
<li>civil protection services.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This is extremely similar to the 2011 rules, which inherited most concepts.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>3. Duty to Contribute to Public Revenues (Taxes)</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Article 70/I</strong></p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This article:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>required all citizens to contribute to public revenues,</li>
<li>used the specific phrase “<strong>according to their income and wealth</strong>,”</li>
<li>grounding taxation in a notion of fairness based on economic capacity.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Unlike the 2011 law, there was <strong>no mention of child-rearing</strong> or personal circumstances.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>4. Duty of Parents to Ensure Children’s Education</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Article 70/J</strong></p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This article imposed an explicit, enforceable duty on:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>parents or legal guardians<br/>to ensure the <strong>education of minor children</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This parental duty is now addressed mainly in the Child Protection Act, not the constitution itself.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>5. Obligation to Resist Unconstitutional Seizure of Power</strong></h3>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Article 2(3)</strong></p>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">One of the most striking duties of the old constitution:</p>
<blockquote class="wp-block-quote is-layout-flow wp-block-quote-is-layout-flow">
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">If someone attempts to seize power by unconstitutional means, every citizen and body of the state <strong>has the right and obligation to resist</strong>.</p>
</blockquote>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This reflected both:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>post-communist anti-coup anxiety (after 1989), and</li>
<li>a belief that constitutional order must be actively defended by the population.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The current Fundamental Law does <strong>not</strong> include a similar citizen obligation.</p>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h1 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>IV. Structural and Ideological Differences</strong></h1>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>1. The 1949 Constitution</strong> emphasized:</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>civic unity against anti-constitutional threats,</li>
<li>parents’ duty for education,</li>
<li>socialist-derived expectations of service and loyalty,</li>
<li>tax fairness based on wealth/income,</li>
<li>classic conscription.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>2. The 2011 Fundamental Law</strong> emphasizes:</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>sovereignty and national defense,</li>
<li>environmental stewardship,</li>
<li>shared responsibility across generations,</li>
<li>family policy (child-rearing considered in contributions),</li>
<li>general personal responsibility.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The shift reflects:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>conservative, national, and family-oriented values,</li>
<li>constitutional identity linked to Christian heritage,</li>
<li>a modern sustainability-based framework.</li>
</ul>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h1 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>V. Complete Summary List</strong></h1>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>A. Duties under the Fundamental Law (2011)</strong></h2>
<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li>Duty to defend the country (Art. XXXI)</li>
<li>Duty to provide military, unarmed, or civil service during emergencies</li>
<li>Duty to contribute to public needs (taxes), adjusted for child-rearing (Art. XXX)</li>
<li>Duty to protect environment, natural and cultural resources (Art. P)</li>
<li>General responsibility for oneself and contribution to community tasks (Art. N)</li>
</ol>
<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>B. Duties under the 1949 Constitution</strong></h2>
<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li>Duty to defend the country (Art. 70/H)</li>
<li>Duty to perform emergency work or material services during crises</li>
<li>Duty to contribute to public revenues according to income and wealth (Art. 70/I)</li>
<li>Duty of parents to ensure children’s education (Art. 70/J)</li>
<li>Duty to resist unconstitutional seizure of power (Art. 2(3))</li>
</ol>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h1 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>VI. Concluding Interpretation</strong></h1>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The <strong>Fundamental Law</strong> modernizes traditional constitutional duties by:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>framing obligations as part of community responsibility,</li>
<li>adding strong environmental and generational duties,</li>
<li>integrating family policy into taxation responsibilities,</li>
<li>and focusing on national defense as a collective value.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The <strong>old constitution</strong> emphasized:</p>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>protection of constitutional order,</li>
<li>social and economic fairness in taxation,</li>
<li>parental responsibility,</li>
<li>and classic civic obligations (defense, education, emergency service).</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Overall, the new constitutional framework places <strong>more emphasis on national identity and intergenerational responsibility</strong>, while the old one had <strong>more explicit civic duties directed toward the preservation of democratic order and social welfare systems</strong>.</p>
