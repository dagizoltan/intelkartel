<h3 class="wp-block-heading">What is Protection Money?</h3>



<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph"><strong>Protection money</strong> is a payment made by individuals or businesses, typically to a criminal organization, in exchange for protection from potential harm, violence, or other criminal activities. This arrangement is often coercive, where the payer is essentially being extorted to avoid threats or attacks from the same group offering “protection.”</p>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">There are <strong>legal</strong> and <strong>illegal</strong> versions of protection payments, although they manifest in different ways and operate under distinct frameworks.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">1. <strong>Illegal Protection Money (Extortion)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This type of protection money is paid to <strong>organized crime groups</strong> (e.g., mafia, street gangs, cartels) under duress or coercion. It’s extortion, where the criminals claim to protect businesses or individuals from harm, while in reality, they are the ones threatening violence if payments aren’t made.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Who Pays?</h4>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Small businesses</strong>: Restaurants, bars, local shops, and other small establishments are common targets.</li>



<li><strong>Street vendors</strong>: Especially in areas with high crime or poor law enforcement, street vendors might be forced to pay.</li>



<li><strong>Construction companies</strong>: In regions controlled by organized crime, construction and real estate developers might be pressured to pay for protection.</li>



<li><strong>Corporations</strong>: In some corrupt countries or regions, even larger corporations may pay off gangs or militias to avoid sabotage or attacks on their supply chains.</li>
</ul>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Why Do They Pay?</h4>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Fear of violence</strong>: Businesses often pay to avoid physical harm or damage to their property.</li>



<li><strong>Avoiding sabotage</strong>: Criminal groups may sabotage equipment or interrupt business operations if payment is not made.</li>



<li><strong>Lack of police protection</strong>: In areas with weak law enforcement, businesses may not have alternatives and find it safer to comply.</li>
</ul>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Market Price for Illegal Protection</h4>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Varies widely by region and threat level</strong>. For small businesses in poor areas, it could be a few hundred dollars per month. For large companies or high-profile targets, payments could run into the thousands or even millions annually.</li>



<li>In regions like Italy, where the Mafia still operates, extortion (or “pizzo”) payments can be <strong>2-3% of gross income</strong>.</li>



<li>In some Latin American countries, payments may range from <strong>$100 to $1,000 a month</strong> depending on the business’s size.</li>
</ul>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">2. <strong>Legal Forms of “Protection Money”</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">While outright extortion is illegal, there are forms of <strong>legal protection payments</strong> that operate under legal frameworks but can still feel coercive to some individuals or businesses.</p>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Who Pays?</h4>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Companies in regions with unstable governments</strong>: In conflict zones, companies might pay governments, warlords, or militias for security.</li>



<li><strong>Businesses needing private security</strong>: High-net-worth individuals or companies in regions with high crime might hire private security forces or bodyguards.</li>



<li><strong>Businesses involved in corruption</strong>: In some countries, businesses may pay off police or local officials for protection against competitors or to avoid harassment.</li>
</ul>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Why Do They Pay?</h4>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Regulatory avoidance</strong>: Companies may pay bribes or corrupt officials to bypass regulations or avoid inspections.</li>



<li><strong>Protection from lawsuits</strong>: Some companies may pay off officials to avoid legal action or to settle disputes quickly.</li>



<li><strong>Ensure operations in conflict zones</strong>: When operating in dangerous regions, companies might pay local militias or even government forces to ensure their assets and employees are safe.</li>
</ul>



<h4 class="wp-block-heading">Market Price for Legal Protection</h4>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Private security contracts</strong>: These can be substantial. A single security officer in a developed country might cost $30,000 to $60,000 annually.</li>



<li><strong>Bribes and corruption-related payments</strong>: Harder to quantify, but in highly corrupt regions, payments to local officials or police may range from a few thousand to millions depending on the size of the business.</li>



<li>In conflict zones, companies often pay <strong>militias or local power brokers</strong> upwards of <strong>hundreds of thousands or even millions of dollars</strong> annually to secure business operations.</li>
</ul>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Examples from Around the World:</h3>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Italy (Mafia &amp; Pizzo)</strong>:</li>
</ol>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>In southern Italy, businesses pay the Mafia a “pizzo,” a form of protection racket. The price varies by the size of the business but usually constitutes a percentage of earnings (2-3%).</li>
</ul>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Mexico (Cartels)</strong>:</li>
</ol>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Mexican drug cartels often extort local businesses for protection money. These payments can range from a few hundred dollars to thousands per month, depending on the business size.</li>
</ul>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Russia</strong>:</li>
</ol>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>In Russia during the 1990s and early 2000s, protection rackets were rampant. Businesspeople had to pay “krysha” (roof) to criminal groups or corrupt government officials in exchange for protection from violence or harassment.</li>
</ul>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Afghanistan (Taliban)</strong>:</li>
</ol>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Companies operating in conflict zones like Afghanistan often pay the Taliban or local militias to ensure their workers and property are not attacked. This form of protection can cost millions.</li>
</ul>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Key Differences:</h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Illegal payments</strong> are involuntary and coerced, often made to criminal groups under threat of violence.</li>



<li><strong>Legal payments</strong> may involve hiring security or, in corrupt environments, paying bribes, but they operate under a veil of legitimacy or legality.</li>
</ul>



<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">The “market price” for protection money, both legal and illegal, varies significantly based on location, the threat of violence, the business’s size, and whether criminal or legitimate actors are involved.</p>
