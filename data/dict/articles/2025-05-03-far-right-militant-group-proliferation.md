<h3 class="wp-block-heading">How Far-Right Militant Groups Multiply and Embed Themselves</h3>
<h4 class="wp-block-heading">1. <strong>Decentralized Structure (“Independent Nodes”)</strong></h4>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>These groups often adopt a <strong>cell-based or “leaderless resistance” model</strong>, where small units operate independently. This makes them <strong>harder to detect and disrupt</strong>.</li>
<li>Online platforms and encrypted communications help form <strong>“virtual cells”</strong> that are ideologically aligned but not formally connected.</li>
</ul>
<h4 class="wp-block-heading">2. <strong>Support from Rogue or Authoritarian States</strong></h4>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Some regimes <strong>tolerate or even support</strong> far-right militias because they can <strong>suppress opposition, immigrants, minorities, or leftist movements</strong>.</li>
<li>Governments may <strong>turn a blind eye</strong>, indirectly <strong>empower</strong> them through rhetoric, or <strong>embed them within state structures</strong> (e.g. police, military, paramilitary groups).</li>
</ul>
<h4 class="wp-block-heading">3. <strong>Infiltration of Institutions</strong></h4>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>These groups often aim to <strong>infiltrate police, military, and local government</strong> to gain access to weapons, training, and legitimacy.</li>
<li><strong>Sympathetic officials</strong> may help cover up their activities or weaken enforcement against them.</li>
</ul>
<h4 class="wp-block-heading">4. <strong>Social Camouflage</strong></h4>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>They run charities, cultural groups, or even political parties as <strong>fronts</strong>.</li>
<li>They target <strong>disaffected youth, veterans, and nationalists</strong>, using <strong>propaganda and identity politics</strong> to recruit and radicalize.</li>
</ul>
<h4 class="wp-block-heading">5. <strong>Propaganda and Recruitment</strong></h4>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Through social media, memes, conspiracy theories (like “Great Replacement”), and cultural grievances, they build a <strong>shared narrative</strong>.</li>
<li>They frame themselves as <strong>defenders of the nation</strong>, using <strong>fear and nostalgia</strong> to build appeal.</li>
</ul>
<h4 class="wp-block-heading">6. <strong>Militarization and Training</strong></h4>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>They hold <strong>paramilitary training camps</strong>, often framed as survivalist or patriotic events.</li>
<li>Some gain <strong>combat experience abroad</strong> (e.g. Ukraine, Syria) and return home with skills and connections.</li>
</ul>
<h4 class="wp-block-heading">7. <strong>“Multiplying Like Flies” — Exponential Spread</strong></h4>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Once they establish a few successful cells, they inspire others, especially <strong>lone actors</strong> who see themselves as part of a larger war.</li>
<li>They thrive in <strong>chaotic environments</strong>, such as during economic crises or political unrest.</li>
</ul>
