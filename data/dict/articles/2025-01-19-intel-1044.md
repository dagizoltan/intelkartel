<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">Estimating the number of spies who have died in the last 70 years in Europe, the USA, and Russia is extremely difficult due to the secretive nature of espionage activities. However, we can make some general observations and rough estimates based on historical patterns, documented cases, and known events.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Key Contextual Factors</strong></h3>
<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Cold War Era (1947–1991):</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Espionage activities between the West (led by the USA and NATO) and the Eastern Bloc (led by the Soviet Union) were at their peak.</li>
<li>Known cases include assassinations (e.g., the KGB’s assassination of defectors) and executions of captured spies.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Post-Cold War Era (1991–Present):</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Espionage has continued, though less overtly. Cyberespionage and economic espionage have also grown significantly.</li>
<li>Political assassinations, such as those involving nerve agents or poison, have remained a method of targeting spies and defectors.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Estimate for Each Region</strong></h3>
<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Russia (Soviet Union and Post-Soviet Russia):</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Many spies (real or suspected) were executed during Stalin’s purges in the 1930s and 1940s, but in the last 70 years, executions and assassinations still occurred.</li>
<li>Notable examples include:
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Execution of Soviet defectors like Oleg Penkovsky (1963).</li>
<li>Assassinations abroad, such as Alexander Litvinenko (2006) and attempts on Sergei Skripal (2018).</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Estimated deaths: <strong>1,000–3,000 spies</strong> (including both domestic purges and foreign operations).</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>Europe:</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Western European countries were active during the Cold War in counterintelligence and espionage.</li>
<li>Nazi Germany executed hundreds of spies during World War II, but in the last 70 years, the numbers have declined significantly.</li>
<li>Deaths include agents caught by Eastern Bloc countries and assassinations carried out by various intelligence services (e.g., Mossad operations in Europe targeting individuals linked to terrorism or espionage).</li>
<li>Estimated deaths: <strong>500–1,500 spies</strong>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li><strong>United States:</strong>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>The U.S. executed notable Soviet spies, such as Julius and Ethel Rosenberg (1953), though cases of executions are rare.</li>
<li>Many spies may have died during covert operations or under mysterious circumstances, but the U.S. tends to capture rather than kill foreign agents.</li>
<li>Estimated deaths: <strong>200–500 spies</strong>.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Total Estimate:</strong></h3>
<p class="has-text-align-justify wp-block-paragraph">Over the past 70 years, approximately <strong>2,000 to 5,000 spies</strong> may have died globally in Europe, the USA, and Russia due to assassinations, executions, or covert operations. This number remains speculative and heavily dependent on declassified information and historical accounts. Many deaths likely remain unknown due to the classified nature of espionage activities.</p>
