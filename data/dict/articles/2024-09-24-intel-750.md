<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Germany’s immigrant population is diverse, drawing from both European Union (EU) countries and non-EU regions. Here’s a more detailed breakdown of where many immigrants in Germany come from as of 2023-2024:</p>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>European Union (EU) Countries</strong>:</li>
</ol>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Romania</strong>: Approximately <strong>851,000 people</strong>. Many have migrated for work opportunities, benefiting from free movement within the EU.</li>



<li><strong>Poland</strong>: About <strong>866,000 individuals</strong>, also part of EU migration flows.</li>



<li><strong>Italy</strong>: Around <strong>640,000 people</strong>, contributing to Germany’s workforce, particularly in sectors like hospitality and manufacturing.</li>



<li><strong>Bulgaria</strong>: Over <strong>400,000 individuals</strong>, benefiting from the EU labor mobility laws.</li>



<li><strong>Croatia</strong>: Around <strong>300,000 people</strong> following Croatia’s EU membership.</li>
</ul>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Non-EU Countries</strong>:</li>
</ol>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Turkey</strong>: The largest non-EU group, with <strong>1.55 million</strong> individuals, many of whom are descendants of “guest workers” invited to Germany in the 1960s.</li>



<li><strong>Syria</strong>: Over <strong>876,000 people</strong>, primarily refugees from the Syrian civil war.</li>



<li><strong>Ukraine</strong>: An influx of <strong>1.1 million Ukrainians</strong> due to the ongoing conflict, particularly after the 2022 Russian invasion.</li>



<li><strong>Russia</strong>: Around <strong>263,000 people</strong>, many of whom migrated during earlier waves of Russian emigration.</li>
</ul>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Other Middle Eastern and Asian Countries</strong>:</li>
</ol>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Afghanistan</strong>: Over <strong>320,000 people</strong>, many of whom are refugees fleeing conflict and political instability.</li>



<li><strong>Iraq</strong>: Significant numbers of refugees from Iraq, contributing to the Middle Eastern diaspora in Germany.</li>
</ul>



<ol class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>African and Other Regions</strong>:</li>
</ol>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li>Immigrants from African countries such as <strong>Morocco</strong> and <strong>Tunisia</strong> also contribute to the population, although in smaller numbers compared to the Middle East and EU nations.</li>
</ul>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Key Immigration Trends:</h3>



<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Asylum Seekers</strong>: Germany has seen large waves of asylum seekers, particularly from Syria, Afghanistan, and Ukraine.</li>



<li><strong>EU Migration</strong>: Germany remains a popular destination for workers from Eastern Europe, especially from countries like Romania and Poland.</li>



<li><strong>Labor Migration</strong>: Many non-EU nationals come to Germany for employment opportunities, particularly in industries like construction, manufacturing, and health care.</li>
</ul>
