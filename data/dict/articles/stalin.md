<h2 class="wp-block-heading">MAJOR ESSAYS IN STALIN’S <em>COLLECTED WORKS</em></h2>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">(one paragraph each, concise, high-yield)</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Anarchism or Socialism? (1906–1907)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In this essay, Stalin criticizes anarchism and defends Marxist socialism. He argues that anarchists reject the need for organization, discipline, and the state, which makes their ideology impractical for achieving socialism. Stalin maintains that only a centralized, disciplined party representing the working class can overthrow capitalism and build socialism. The essay establishes his early belief in authority, hierarchy, and party control.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Marxism and the National Question (1913)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin defines a nation as a historically formed community with a common language, territory, economic life, and culture. He rejects both extreme nationalism and cultural autonomy, arguing that national movements must be subordinated to class struggle. The essay supports centralized state control while allowing limited national expression, and it became the foundation of Soviet nationality policy.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The October Revolution and the Tactics of the Russian Communists (1924)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin explains why the Bolsheviks succeeded in the October Revolution while socialist movements failed elsewhere. He emphasizes correct leadership, party discipline, and the alliance between workers and peasants. The essay justifies the Bolshevik seizure of power and presents centralized strategy as essential for revolutionary success.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Foundations of Leninism (1924)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This work presents Stalin’s interpretation of Lenin’s ideas after Lenin’s death. He defines Leninism as Marxism adapted to imperialism and revolution in one country. The essay emphasizes the leading role of the Communist Party, the dictatorship of the proletariat, and strict discipline. It also serves to legitimize Stalin’s authority as Lenin’s ideological successor.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Trotskyism or Leninism? (1924)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin attacks Leon Trotsky by portraying him as anti-Leninist and ideologically inconsistent. He accuses Trotsky of underestimating the peasantry and rejecting party unity. The essay is both ideological and political, aimed at eliminating Trotsky as a rival and strengthening Stalin’s control over the party.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Concerning Questions of Leninism (1926)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This essay expands Stalin’s theory of Leninism and reinforces the need for centralized authority. Stalin defends the idea of “socialism in one country,” arguing that the USSR could build socialism independently of world revolution. The work further marginalizes opposition and establishes Stalinism as official doctrine.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Socialism in One Country (mid-1920s writings)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Across several essays, Stalin argues that socialism can be built within the USSR alone, even without immediate global revolution. This directly contradicts Trotsky’s theory of permanent revolution. The concept justified focusing on internal development, industrialization, and consolidation of power under Stalin’s leadership.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>On the Opposition (1928)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin condemns internal party opposition as dangerous and counterrevolutionary. He argues that dissent weakens socialism and aids class enemies. The essay justifies expulsions, repression, and the elimination of alternative viewpoints within the Communist Party.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Problems of Agrarian Policy in the USSR (late 1920s)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin defends collectivization as necessary to eliminate the kulaks and modernize agriculture. He claims collectivization strengthens socialism and secures grain supplies for industrialization. The essay ignores the human cost and frames resistance as sabotage.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Report to the Fifteenth Party Congress (1927)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin outlines the need for rapid industrialization and increased party discipline. He argues that the USSR faces capitalist encirclement and must strengthen itself economically and militarily. This report prepares the ideological ground for the Five-Year Plans.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Report to the Sixteenth Party Congress (1930)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin praises the success of the First Five-Year Plan and defends forced collectivization. He dismisses failures as the result of sabotage and enemy activity. The report reinforces authoritarian control and economic centralization.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Report to the Seventeenth Party Congress (1934)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin declares that socialism has been achieved “in the main” in the USSR. He claims exploiting classes have been eliminated and the economy transformed. Despite its optimistic tone, the report stresses vigilance against enemies and precedes the Great Purge.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Dialectical and Historical Materialism (1938)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This essay presents a simplified version of Marxist philosophy. Stalin explains dialectical materialism as the official worldview of the Soviet state. It became mandatory doctrine in education and justified political decisions as scientifically inevitable.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>On the Draft Constitution of the USSR (1936)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin presents the new Soviet Constitution as democratic and progressive. He claims it guarantees rights and reflects socialist success. In reality, the essay masks increasing repression and centralized power.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The Great Patriotic War Speeches (1941–1945)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In wartime speeches, Stalin appeals to patriotism rather than pure ideology. He frames the war as a defense of the Soviet motherland and mobilizes national unity. These speeches helped legitimize his leadership during WWII.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Economic Problems of Socialism in the USSR (1952)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin’s final major theoretical work addresses socialist economics. He argues that commodity production still exists under socialism but must be controlled by the state. The essay attempts to adapt Marxist theory to Soviet practice and reinforces central planning.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">ADDITIONAL ESSAYS FROM <em>COLLECTED WORKS</em></h2>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The Party Before and After Taking Power (1921)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin argues that the Communist Party must change its methods after seizing power. While secrecy and flexibility were necessary before the revolution, strict discipline and centralized administration are required afterward. The essay justifies bureaucratic expansion and increased control as necessary for governing a socialist state.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The Results of the Work of the Fourteenth Conference of the RCP(B) (1925)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin outlines the Party’s achievements and defends its economic direction. He emphasizes industrial growth and party unity while dismissing opposition criticism. The essay reinforces his growing dominance within the party leadership.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The International Character of the October Revolution (1927)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin argues that the October Revolution had global significance but insists that socialism can still be built within one country. He balances internationalism with national development, further opposing Trotsky’s theory of permanent revolution.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The Right Deviation in the CPSU(B) (1929)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">This essay attacks Bukharin and other “Right Opposition” figures. Stalin accuses them of slowing collectivization and industrialization. It provides ideological justification for removing moderate leaders and intensifying economic coercion.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Year of the Great Break (1929)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin declares that the USSR has entered a decisive turning point toward socialism. He frames rapid industrialization and collectivization as historic necessities. The essay celebrates speed and force while dismissing social costs.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Dizzy with Success (1930)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin temporarily criticizes excesses in collectivization, blaming local officials rather than policy. He presents himself as a corrector of mistakes while maintaining the overall course. The essay helped stabilize rural unrest without reversing collectivization.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Reply to Collective Farm Comrades (1930)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In response to peasant complaints, Stalin defends collectivization and insists abuses are temporary. He reassures peasants while reaffirming state authority. The essay reinforces compliance through controlled concessions.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>On Industrialization and the Grain Problem (early 1930s)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin links agricultural extraction to industrial growth. He argues that grain procurement is essential to build heavy industry and defend the USSR. Resistance is labeled sabotage, justifying coercive measures.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>On the Tasks of Economic Executives (1931)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin addresses factory managers and planners, demanding discipline, efficiency, and obedience. He stresses technical expertise combined with political loyalty. The essay promotes centralized economic command.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>On Some Questions Concerning the History of Bolshevism (1931)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin rewrites party history to emphasize his role and marginalize rivals. He presents ideological unity as historically necessary. This essay marks the beginning of systematic historical falsification.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Speech at the First All-Union Conference of Stakhanovites (1935)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin praises model workers to promote productivity. He frames labor enthusiasm as socialist consciousness. The essay supports propaganda campaigns to raise output.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>On the Draft Constitution of the USSR (1936)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">(Expanded emphasis)<br>Stalin presents the constitution as proof that socialism has triumphed. He claims universal suffrage and rights reflect equality under socialism. In reality, the essay conceals intensified repression and centralized authority.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>On Shortcomings in Party Work and Measures for Liquidating Trotskyites and Other Double-Dealers (1937)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin justifies mass purges by accusing enemies of infiltration. He frames repression as defensive necessity. The essay provides ideological cover for the Great Terror.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>On Dialectical and Historical Materialism (1938)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">(Philosophical reinforcement)<br>Stalin presents Marxism as a scientific law of history. He reduces philosophy to simplified principles usable for governance. This text became mandatory ideological doctrine.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Speech on the Great Patriotic War (1941)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin shifts rhetoric from class struggle to national survival. He invokes Russian history and patriotism to mobilize resistance. This marked a strategic ideological adjustment during WWII.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>On the Post-War Development of the USSR (late 1940s)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Stalin argues for reconstruction under strict planning. He stresses military strength and ideological vigilance. The essay reflects Cold War tensions and internal control.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Economic Problems of Socialism in the USSR (1952)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">(Expanded emphasis)<br>Stalin insists that economic laws still operate under socialism but must be consciously managed. He rejects decentralization and reinforces state planning. This was his final attempt to define orthodox Stalinist economics.</p>



<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity">



<h2 class="wp-block-heading">STALIN — ONE-LINE MEMORY TRIGGERS</h2>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Anarchism or Socialism?</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Anarchism = chaos; socialism needs a disciplined party and strong state.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Marxism and the National Question</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">A nation = language, territory, economy, culture; nationalism must obey class struggle.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The October Revolution and the Tactics of the Russian Communists</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Bolsheviks won because of correct leadership, discipline, and worker–peasant alliance.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Foundations of Leninism</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Leninism = Marxism adapted to imperialism; party leadership is supreme.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Trotskyism or Leninism?</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Trotsky is anti-Lenin; party unity over individual leaders.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Concerning Questions of Leninism</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Defends socialism in one country and crushes ideological deviation.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Socialism in One Country</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">USSR can build socialism alone without world revolution.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>On the Opposition</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Internal dissent equals danger to socialism and must be eliminated.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Problems of Agrarian Policy in the USSR</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Collectivization is necessary; kulaks are class enemies.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Report to the Fifteenth Party Congress</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Rapid industrialization is essential due to capitalist encirclement.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Report to the Sixteenth Party Congress</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Five-Year Plan success proves correctness of forced policies.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Report to the Seventeenth Party Congress</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Socialism achieved “in the main,” but vigilance against enemies remains.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Year of the Great Break</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">1929 marks the decisive turn to full socialism through force and speed.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Dizzy with Success</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Excesses blamed on officials, not policy; collectivization continues.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Reply to Collective Farm Comrades</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Peasant resistance dismissed; collectivization reaffirmed.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The Right Deviation in the CPSU(B)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Bukharin and moderates are enemies slowing socialism.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>On Industrialization and the Grain Problem</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Grain extraction funds industry; resistance is sabotage.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>On the Tasks of Economic Executives</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Managers must obey the plan and party discipline.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The Party Before and After Taking Power</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Revolutionary flexibility ends; bureaucratic discipline begins.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>On Some Questions Concerning the History of Bolshevism</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Party history rewritten to justify Stalin’s leadership.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Speech at the Stakhanovite Conference</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Model workers prove socialist enthusiasm raises productivity.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>On the Draft Constitution of the USSR</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Constitution claims democracy while power stays centralized.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>On Shortcomings in Party Work (1937)</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Purges justified as defense against hidden enemies.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Dialectical and Historical Materialism</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Marxism reduced to official, unquestionable state philosophy.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Great Patriotic War Speeches</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Patriotism replaces class rhetoric to mobilize the nation.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>On Post-War Development</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Reconstruction through planning and ideological vigilance.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Economic Problems of Socialism in the USSR</strong></h3>



<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Markets exist under socialism but must be controlled by the state.</p>
