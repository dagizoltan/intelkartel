<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Why don’t they go to a psychologist?</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Normal Development</strong>: Childhood crushes and early feelings of love are considered normal parts of growing up. Children naturally go through phases of attachment, admiration, and affection for others, which help them learn about relationships.</li>
<li><strong>Not Seen as a Problem</strong>: These early feelings are usually harmless and don’t interfere with the child’s life or well-being, so they don’t raise concerns for parents or teachers that would warrant psychological intervention.</li>
<li><strong>Lack of Emotional Depth</strong>: Crushes at a young age are typically not deep or complex. Children may not even recognize these feelings as “love” in the adult sense, so there’s no strong need to process them with a professional.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Why does the love not last long?</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Emotional Immaturity</strong>: At a young age, children’s understanding of emotions and relationships is limited. They don’t yet have the emotional tools to sustain deep, long-term romantic connections.</li>
<li><strong>Rapid Development</strong>: Both children and teenagers go through rapid changes in their interests, friendships, and even personalities. What seems like intense affection one day may fade quickly as they grow and experience new things.</li>
<li><strong>Exploration and Curiosity</strong>: Early crushes are often based on curiosity or admiration rather than genuine romantic love. As children move through adolescence, they learn more about their own preferences and feelings, which leads to new and different attractions.</li>
</ul>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Why does it come back later, if ever?</h3>
<ul class="wp-block-list">
<li><strong>Development of Deeper Emotional Capacity</strong>: As people age, especially after puberty, their emotional understanding deepens, and they become capable of more meaningful, long-lasting love. They develop empathy, communication skills, and a clearer sense of self.</li>
<li><strong>Hormonal Changes</strong>: Puberty, which typically starts around age 12 for boys, triggers significant hormonal changes that contribute to sexual attraction and romantic feelings. This is why love feels more intense or “real” during and after adolescence.</li>
<li><strong>Nostalgia and Reflection</strong>: Some people later in life might remember their childhood crushes with fondness, especially if the feelings were tied to positive experiences. Sometimes, people reconnect with those early feelings, but usually, love evolves into something more mature and complex as people grow older.</li>
</ul>
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The process of falling in and out of love in childhood and adolescence is a natural part of psychological development. Young people are learning about relationships, emotions, and attachment, and this evolving process leads to deeper, more lasting forms of love later on.</p>
